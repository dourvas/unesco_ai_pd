Individual teachers making thoughtful decisions in their own classrooms is necessary — but not sufficient. The students who benefit most from ethical AI education are the ones whose entire school community has a shared, coherent approach. That requires policy. That requires leadership. That requires educators willing to move from personal practice to institutional change.

This module is about creating that change.

🏫 Part 1: From Classroom Practice to Institutional Policy

A school AI policy is not a bureaucratic document — it is a shared ethical commitment. When done well, it protects students, supports teachers, and builds a community where AI is used purposefully and responsibly.

Why Individual Practice Is Not Enough

Without shared institutional guidelines, students encounter a different AI policy in every classroom. This creates confusion, inconsistency, and inequity — particularly for students who most need clear, stable expectations.

Without school policy:
  • Each teacher invents their own rules
  • Students learn to game inconsistency
  • Vulnerable students fall through the gaps
  • Teachers bear all the burden alone
  • No appeal process when disputes arise
With school policy:
  • Shared expectations, consistent enforcement
  • Students know what is and isn't permitted
  • Equity built into the framework
  • Teachers supported by institutional backing
  • Fair process for disputes and appeals

📋 Part 2: Seven Elements of Effective School AI Policy

Research and practice converge on seven core elements that effective school AI policies share. Each is necessary; none is sufficient alone.

1
Clear Definitions

The policy specifies what counts as "AI use" — including text generation, image generation, translation tools, grammar assistants, and code completion. Vague policies create loopholes and disputes.

2
Differentiated Expectations by Context

The policy acknowledges that appropriate AI use varies by subject, task type, and learning goal. It provides guidance for different contexts rather than a single blanket rule.

3
Transparency and Disclosure Procedures

The policy establishes how and when students disclose AI use — standardized forms, assignment-level instructions, or portfolio documentation. Disclosure is framed as a skill and a responsibility, not a trap.

4
Equity and Access Provisions

The policy addresses what happens when not all students have equal access to AI tools. It includes provisions for in-school access, alternative assessments, and accessibility accommodations.

5
Data Privacy and Tool Approval Process

The policy specifies which AI tools are approved for student use and why — including GDPR compliance review. Teachers and students do not use unapproved tools with student data.

6
Integrity Procedures and Due Process

The policy outlines what happens when suspected violations occur — including the steps before any formal action, the student's right to respond, and an appeals process. No AI detection score alone is sufficient basis for a finding of misconduct.

7
Review and Update Cycle

AI capabilities change rapidly. Effective policies include a scheduled review cycle — at minimum annually — and a process for teachers to flag issues as they arise. A policy written in 2024 may be outdated by 2025.

🤝 Part 3: How to Develop Policy That Actually Works

Policy developed by administrators and handed to teachers rarely works. Policy developed with teachers, students, and parents is more likely to reflect real needs and earn genuine buy-in.

A Five-Step Participatory Process

Step 1
Audit current practice. What AI tools are already being used in your school — by teachers and students? What informal rules exist? Where are the gaps and inconsistencies? Use the AI Tool Evaluation Checklist to assess current tools.
Step 2
Consult all stakeholders. Run structured conversations with teachers across subjects, students (especially older ones), and parents. What concerns do they have? What support do they need? What would make them feel confident?
Step 3
Draft with a small working group. Include at least one teacher from each major subject area, a student representative (secondary level), and a parent. Use the School AI Policy Template as your starting framework.
Step 4
Pilot and review. Implement the draft policy for one semester. Collect feedback systematically. What is working? What is creating problems? What needs clarification?
Step 5
Communicate and embed. Share the final policy with the whole school community — parents via newsletter, students via handbook, teachers via professional development. Make it a living document, not a filing cabinet item.

⚡ Part 5: Special Circumstances — When the Standard Approach Doesn't Apply

Ethical AI policy must account for students and situations that fall outside the norm. The following cases require specific consideration in any school-wide framework.

Emergency Situations

When a student faces a personal crisis — bereavement, illness, family emergency — standard integrity expectations may need to be temporarily adjusted. The question is not "did they follow the rules?" but "how do we support this student's learning while maintaining fairness to peers?"

Policy principle: Emergency extensions and alternative assessments are always preferable to either penalizing the student or quietly waiving standards. Document decisions consistently so similar situations receive similar treatment.

Students with IEPs, 504 Plans, or Learning Differences

AI tools can be powerful accessibility supports — text-to-speech, grammar assistance, organizational tools — that level the playing field for students with learning differences. These uses should be explicitly addressed in both the school AI policy and individual student plans.

Policy principle: Accessibility-oriented AI use is not an exception to the integrity policy — it is an expression of the inclusion principle. Consult with special education colleagues when designing AI policies to ensure these students are not disadvantaged by overly broad restrictions.

High-Stakes and Standardized Assessment

External examinations typically have their own rules about AI use — which may be stricter or different from school policy. Teachers need to prepare students for these contexts explicitly, including the skills of working without AI assistance.

Policy principle: Classroom AI integration should include regular practice at tasks completed without AI, so students remain capable and confident in high-stakes contexts where AI is not permitted.

🎓 Part 6: Age-Appropriate AI Ethics — Across the School

Ethical AI education looks different at different developmental stages. A school-wide approach must be coherent — but not uniform. The following provides a framework for differentiated implementation:

🌱 Early Childhood & Primary

Ages 5–11

  • Focus on what AI is — a tool made by people, with limitations
  • Distinguish between AI output and human creativity
  • Introduce the concept of honesty in work
  • No independent AI use without teacher supervision

🌿 Lower Secondary

Ages 12–15

  • Develop critical evaluation of AI outputs
  • Practice disclosure routines
  • Explore ethical dilemmas through discussion
  • Begin to understand bias, privacy, equity

🌳 Upper Secondary

Ages 16–18

  • Apply ethical frameworks to complex cases
  • Contribute to classroom AI policy discussions
  • Develop personal AI use philosophy
  • Prepare for AI use norms in higher education and work

❓ Part 7: Advanced FAQ — Complex and Edge Cases

These questions represent the harder end of AI ethics in education — cases that require institutional frameworks, not just individual judgment.

What do we do when a student claims an emergency to explain suspected AI misuse?

Treat the emergency claim and the integrity concern as separate issues. Address the student's wellbeing first: if there is a genuine emergency, provide support and an alternative assessment pathway.

If you still have integrity concerns after the emergency is addressed, handle them through your standard process — with the same evidence standards and due process as any other case. Do not use the emergency as a reason to either overlook the concern or to treat the student more harshly.

How do we handle AI use in subjects where it is genuinely hard to define what counts as "the student's own work"?

This is a genuine challenge in creative subjects, project-based learning, and collaborative work. The answer lies in shifting the focus of assessment: rather than asking "is this entirely the student's work?", ask "does this demonstrate the student's learning?"

Design assessments that make learning visible regardless of tool use: oral presentations, process portfolios, in-class components, peer explanation tasks. The student who understands their work can always demonstrate it.

A student with a documented learning difference uses AI tools more extensively than classmates. Other students say this is unfair. How do we respond?

This is a teachable moment about the difference between equality and equity. Equal treatment means everyone gets the same. Equitable treatment means everyone gets what they need to participate meaningfully.

Without disclosing the student's specific needs (which are confidential), you can explain to the class that different students have different support needs — just as some students use reading glasses, some use calculators, and some need extra time. The goal is always the learning, not the restriction.

Our school policy was written before current AI tools existed. How do we update it without starting from scratch?

Use the AI Tool Evaluation Checklist to audit which tools are currently in use and whether they meet current standards. Then convene a small working group to identify where the existing policy is silent, contradictory, or outdated.

Focus updates on: definitions (what counts as AI), disclosure procedures, and tool approval processes. These are the areas most likely to have become obsolete. Use the School AI Policy Template as a reference for what a current policy should include.

What guidance do we give students preparing for university, where AI policies vary widely?

Prepare students to read and follow context-specific policies — not just your school's rules. The skill of understanding and adapting to different institutional expectations is itself an important AI literacy competency.

More fundamentally: students who have developed genuine critical thinking, strong writing skills, and ethical judgment about AI use will navigate any university context successfully — with or without AI assistance.

Transparency and Disclosure Procedures

Guidance: Disclosure should be framed as a professional skill, not a confession. The goal is a culture of honesty — not a surveillance system.

Honest disclosure is treated as evidence of professional maturity, not as grounds for penalty.

4

Equity and Access Provisions

Guidance: Policies that assume all students have equal home access to AI tools create inequity. Address this explicitly.

The school recognises that not all students have equal access to AI tools at home. Where AI use is required or beneficial for a task, teachers will [provide in-school access / offer an alternative non-AI pathway / both].

AI tools used as documented accessibility supports for students with learning differences are explicitly permitted and are not subject to general AI restrictions. These uses are specified in individual student plans.

The school will review whether AI use requirements inadvertently disadvantage any student group as part of the annual policy review.

5

Data Privacy and Tool Approval Process

Guidance: GDPR applies to any AI tool used with student data. "Student data" includes names, work, images, and any identifying information. Unapproved tools must not be used.

Only AI tools that have been reviewed and approved by [designated role, e.g. Data Protection Officer / IT lead / senior leadership] may be used with student data.

The current list of approved tools is maintained at [location: e.g. school intranet / shared drive] and reviewed [frequency: e.g. termly].

Teachers wishing to use a new tool must submit it for approval using the AI Tool Evaluation Checklist before use with students.

Students must not enter personally identifying information into any AI tool, approved or otherwise, without explicit teacher instruction.

6

Integrity Procedures and Due Process

Guidance: No AI detection tool result alone is sufficient to find a student in violation. Always include a dialogue step before any formal action.

When a teacher has concerns about possible undisclosed AI use, the following steps apply:

  1. Conversation first: The teacher speaks privately with the student and asks them to explain their process. No formal action is taken at this stage.
  2. Evidence standard: AI detection tool results are treated as one signal among several — not as conclusive evidence. The student's ability to discuss their work is a key indicator.
  3. Formal process: If the concern persists after conversation, the case is referred to [designated role] and handled according to the school's existing academic integrity procedure.
  4. Right to respond: The student has the right to present their account before any formal finding is recorded.
  5. Appeals: Decisions may be appealed to [designated role / panel] within [X school days].

First-time cases where expectations were unclear are treated as a learning opportunity. Consistent, documented repeat violations are escalated.

7

Review and Update Cycle

Guidance: AI capabilities are changing rapidly. "We will review as needed" is not a review cycle. Set a date and stick to it.

This policy will be formally reviewed on [specific date, e.g. end of each academic year] by [working group / role].

Between formal reviews, any teacher may flag an issue or propose an update by [process: e.g. emailing the designated lead / raising at department meeting]. Urgent issues (e.g. a tool is found to violate GDPR) are addressed immediately.

All changes are version-controlled. The version history is maintained at [location]. Students and parents are notified of significant changes via [channel, e.g. school newsletter / handbook update].

This policy is a living document. The goal is not a perfect text — it is a community that thinks carefully and updates honestly.

6. 🔄 The Designer's Cycle

Designing an AI policy can feel overwhelming. This five-step cycle gives you a concrete starting point — and a structure for returning to your policy as things change. It is a cycle, not a checklist: you will move through it more than once.

1

Identify — What is irreplaceable in my subject?

Name the learning experiences that AI cannot replicate: direct observation, personal voice, live performance, original argument, authentic creation. These are what your policy must protect.

2

Map — Where does AI help and where does it undermine learning?

Go through your curriculum task by task. For each one: does AI support the learning goal, bypass it, or make no difference? This map is the evidence base for your policy decisions.

3

Define — What are the clear rules, task by task?

Write your expectations at the task level — not as a general principle but as specific guidance: "For this assessment, AI use is / is not permitted / requires disclosure." Clarity prevents most problems before they arise.

4

Communicate — How do I explain this to students and parents?

Share your policy explicitly — in writing, before assignments, not after submission. For parents: plain language, focused on learning goals and student wellbeing. For students: the reasoning behind the rules, not just the rules.

5

Review — When will I evaluate whether my policy is working?

Set a specific review date — end of term, end of year. Ask: did the policy support learning? Did it create problems? Has anything changed in AI capabilities that the policy didn't anticipate? Then return to Step 1.

This is a cycle, not a checklist. AI capabilities are developing rapidly. A policy that works today may need revision in six months. The Designer's Cycle is designed to be returned to — not completed once and filed away.
