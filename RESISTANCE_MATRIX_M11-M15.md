# Resistance Matrix — M11–M15 (Create Level)
## Phase 3 of Subject Intro Hooks implementation

**Status:** Phase 3, batch 3 of 3 (M11–M15) — final batch
**Approach:** Y — group thinking, individual cells per subject
**Cells in this batch:** 5 modules × 17 = 85
**Previous batches:** M1–M5 (Acquire), M6–M10 (Deepen) — both approved
**Total cells across Phase 3:** 255 (15 × 17)

---

## How to read this document

Each cell answers three questions for a (subject × module) pair:

- **R** (Resistance) — what this teacher might be silently thinking when they see this module
- **B** (Bridge) — the classroom-recognisable connection that makes the module their territory
- **P** (Promise) — the specific gain this teacher takes from this module

The 17 cells per module are: 16 subjects + 1 Universal fallback.

---

## What changes at the Create level

The shift across UNESCO levels follows a recognisable arc:

| Level | Dominant resistance | Hook strategy |
|---|---|---|
| Acquire | "This isn't for me" | Show relevance |
| Deepen | "Not sure it's worth the effort" | Reduce friction |
| Create | **"Am I qualified to do this?"** | **Lower the bar for action** |

Create-level resistance is imposter syndrome dressed in different clothes per subject. The teacher has absorbed the content (Acquire) and gained confidence in practice (Deepen) — and now is asked to lead, design, share, research, or take a public stance. The voice in their head says "those things are for someone more senior, more confident, more credentialed than me."

The bridges at this level **don't argue that the teacher is qualified.** They reframe the action so that the qualification question doesn't apply. Examples:

- "Lead your colleagues" becomes "share what you've already noticed"
- "Conduct research" becomes "write up something you already did"
- "Develop policy" becomes "draft what you wish your school had done last week"

The promises lower the bar similarly: small first steps, low-risk artefacts, deliverables that don't require permission to produce.

# M11 — Leading Human-Centred AI Practice
## UNESCO Aspect 1, Create Level

**Module focus:** Stewarding human-centred AI culture in your school. Modelling, mentoring colleagues, shaping informal norms. Builds on M1 and M6.

**Generic resistance pattern:** "I'm not in a leadership position. This module assumes a status I don't have." This module faces the strongest imposter response of the Create level because "leadership" sounds positional.

---

### M11 × mathematics
- **R:** I'm not a head of department. Talking about leadership in AI doesn't apply to me.
- **B:** Maths departments often follow whichever colleague is most articulate about AI, regardless of position. If you've thought carefully about AI in maths teaching, you're already shaping departmental norms.
- **P:** Recognition of the informal influence you may already have, with frames for using it intentionally instead of by accident.

### M11 × language_arts
- **R:** Department politics in English are intense. Stepping forward on AI risks taking sides I'd rather not take.
- **B:** Stepping forward isn't the only mode. Modelling — using AI thoughtfully in your own practice and being honest when you do — is leadership without taking public positions.
- **P:** A leadership-by-modelling approach that doesn't require you to argue the AI debate publicly, while still shaping your department's norms.

### M11 × science
- **R:** Science departments respond to evidence, not to leadership rhetoric. Until I have evidence, I shouldn't lead.
- **B:** Evidence-gathering is leadership. The teacher who runs a small classroom inquiry on AI use, shares what they noticed, and invites colleagues to do the same is leading by establishing evidence-norms in the department.
- **P:** A leadership approach grounded in evidence-gathering rather than assertion, suited to science departments and to teachers reluctant to claim expertise.

### M11 × physics
- **R:** Physics teachers are sceptical by training. Most of what's said about AI in education is overclaimed.
- **B:** Sceptical leadership is leadership too — possibly the most needed kind right now. The teacher who reliably distinguishes evidence-backed claims from hype is doing leadership work.
- **P:** A frame for productive scepticism as a leadership stance, not as withdrawal. Useful in departments where someone needs to keep the conversation honest.

### M11 × chemistry
- **R:** Chemistry departments tend to focus on safety and content. AI leadership feels off-topic.
- **B:** Chemistry teachers already lead on safety, which is an analogous form of leadership: setting departmental norms about risks that aren't fully understood. AI raises related questions about new student-facing risks.
- **P:** An extension of the safety-leadership role you already practice, applied to AI-related decisions about student exposure and data.

### M11 × biology
- **R:** I don't want to become the AI-explainer for my department. Too much extra work.
- **B:** Leadership doesn't mean becoming the explainer. It means shaping how the department handles AI when it comes up: who decides, what the criteria are, when to consult specialists. That's a smaller, repeatable role.
- **P:** A leadership pattern that delegates the "explainer" load while keeping the steering: process-shaping rather than content-producing.

### M11 × history
- **R:** Departments make AI decisions through politics. History departments especially. I can't lead through that.
- **B:** Politics is leadable through process. The teacher who proposes a clear decision process — who's consulted, what's documented, when revisions happen — leads even without status.
- **P:** Process-design as leadership, distinct from positional leadership. Suited to teachers who'd rather not enter departmental politics directly.

### M11 × geography
- **R:** I'm new to my department. Leadership talk is premature.
- **B:** Newness is a credible leadership platform on rapidly-changing topics. The teacher who says "I'm new and I'm curious how we should think about this" can convene a conversation that established colleagues would struggle to start.
- **P:** A newness-as-asset frame for AI-related leadership conversations, useful when established colleagues feel locked into prior positions.

### M11 × foreign_languages
- **R:** Language departments are international by nature. AI leadership conversations get tangled in local-vs-global debates fast.
- **B:** Tangled in productive ways. The teacher who maps the local-vs-global tensions clearly — what's being debated, what's at stake, what trade-offs apply — is leading the conversation by clarifying it.
- **P:** A clarification-as-leadership move that doesn't require taking sides, suitable for departments where positions are mixed.

### M11 × social_studies
- **R:** I have strong views on AI's social effects. Leading risks pushing my views on colleagues.
- **B:** The module distinguishes view-pushing from norm-shaping. Leadership-by-norm-shaping focuses on how decisions get made (criteria, evidence, voices), not what gets decided. Your views remain yours.
- **P:** A leadership pattern that protects your views from becoming departmental views, while still influencing how the department operates.

### M11 × computer_science
- **R:** I get asked AI questions constantly already. Adding intentional leadership feels like more of the same.
- **B:** Intentional is the difference. Right now you may be defaulting into a reactive role. The module's frame is choosing what to lead on, what to deflect, what to delegate.
- **P:** A frame for shaping your reactive AI-explainer role into a chosen one, with clear scope and explicit handovers.

### M11 × physical_education
- **R:** PE gets ignored in school-wide AI conversations. I can't lead a conversation I'm not in.
- **B:** PE's exclusion is one of the most important things to surface. The teacher who insists PE is part of the conversation — through brief specific cases, not abstract argument — is leading on inclusion in a department where inclusion gets discussed otherwise.
- **P:** Specific moves to bring PE into school-wide AI conversations, drawing on cases your colleagues won't have considered.

### M11 × arts
- **R:** Art teachers aren't usually asked about technology decisions. We're seen as creative, not strategic.
- **B:** The art teacher's stance on AI in art-making is exactly what other departments need help articulating: where does human authorship live, what counts as the student's work, how do you teach process when the product can be generated. Those questions transfer.
- **P:** Recognition of the leadership your stance offers to colleagues outside the arts, who are working through related questions with less practice.

### M11 × special_education
- **R:** SEN voices are the last consulted on AI. Leadership is for departments with positional influence.
- **B:** SEN consultation late is the school's loss; SEN consultation early is leadership. The teacher who insists "before we adopt this, what about my students?" is leading on inclusion at the planning stage, when it matters.
- **P:** A leadership pattern of front-loading SEN considerations into AI adoption decisions, with explicit moves to claim the chair at that table.

### M11 × early_childhood
- **R:** Early years gets bypassed in AI decisions. We're seen as not-yet-relevant.
- **B:** Bypassed for now, until AI reaches early years (already starting). The teacher who establishes early-years AI norms before the products arrive is leading by precedent, with much less resistance than after.
- **P:** A pre-emptive leadership position for early years, setting norms before the AI-product wave, rather than reacting after.

### M11 × other (Primary Generalist)
- **R:** Primary generalists rarely lead on technology. We're seen as the audience for tech decisions, not the decision-makers.
- **B:** Primary teachers see AI's effects across subjects in ways that secondary specialists can't. That whole-child, whole-curriculum perspective is exactly what AI policy decisions miss when they're left to subject specialists.
- **P:** A leadership claim grounded in primary's unique cross-curricular vantage, useful in school-wide AI conversations dominated by secondary specialist concerns.

### M11 × Universal
- **R:** I don't see myself as a leader. The module title puts me off.
- **B:** Leadership in this module is informal and chosen, not positional and assigned. It's about which conversations you shape and how, not about a job title.
- **P:** A definition of leadership that fits teachers without management positions, and explicit permission to lead small, lead local, lead through example.

# M12 — Designing AI Policy and Classroom Norms
## UNESCO Aspect 2, Create Level

**Module focus:** Drafting school-level and classroom-level AI policy. Acceptable use, transparency expectations, response to misuse. Builds on M2 and M7.

**Generic resistance pattern:** "Policy is the leadership team's job, not mine." Many teachers think policy comes from above and they implement. The module reframes policy-drafting as a teacher's professional skill.

---

### M12 × mathematics
- **R:** I write rules for my classroom; that's not policy. Policy is institutional.
- **B:** Classroom rules become policy when they're shared, debated, and adopted. The maths teacher who drafts a clear AI-on-homework policy, brings it to the department, and lets it spread is doing policy work.
- **P:** A frame for treating your classroom AI rules as policy drafts, rather than personal preferences, which raises their influence within your school.

### M12 × language_arts
- **R:** Writing assessment policy is contested. I don't want my draft to become ammunition.
- **B:** The module's drafting approach foregrounds documentation of trade-offs, not advocacy of positions. A draft that explicitly names what it gives up is harder to weaponise than one that asserts.
- **P:** A drafting style that surfaces trade-offs explicitly, making your policy contributions harder to caricature in departmental conflicts.

### M12 × science
- **R:** Science teaching has stable norms. AI policy on top of them feels like an add-on layer that won't last.
- **B:** Layer is the right word, and modular layers are easier to revise than integrated ones. Drafting AI policy as a separable layer means it can be updated as AI changes without disturbing the stable science-teaching norms underneath.
- **P:** A modular drafting approach that protects existing science teaching norms from AI-related churn, while addressing the new AI-specific decisions.

### M12 × physics
- **R:** Policy without enforcement is theatre. I won't enforce; therefore I shouldn't draft.
- **B:** Enforcement is one mode. The module covers policies that work through transparency and explicit norms rather than through punishment. Different design philosophy.
- **P:** Policy patterns that operate through transparency and explicit expectation-setting rather than through detection and consequence, suited to teachers who don't want to be enforcers.

### M12 × chemistry
- **R:** Chemistry safety policy is well-established. AI policy is a separate domain.
- **B:** Separate domain, related craft. The same drafting moves that produce good safety policy — explicit hazards, clear boundaries, escalation paths — produce good AI policy. The skill transfers.
- **P:** Recognition that you've drafted policy before, in safety, and the same craft applies. Lower learning curve than the topic suggests.

### M12 × biology
- **R:** Policy in my school comes from senior leadership. Drafting from below feels futile.
- **B:** Senior leadership often inherits drafts from below. The teacher whose departmental policy gets adopted, even informally, has effectively contributed to school policy without formal authority.
- **P:** A path from departmental drafting to informal school-level adoption that doesn't require positional authority, useful when waiting for top-down policy isn't an option.

### M12 × history
- **R:** Policy-drafting modules teach generic frameworks. History needs specific cases.
- **B:** The module's frameworks are populated with cases, including history-specific ones: handling AI-generated primary sources, citation-of-AI in essays, evaluation of student-AI conversation in research. Cases are the point, not the framework.
- **P:** History-specific policy cases drafted out, useful as starting points for your own departmental adaptation.

### M12 × geography
- **R:** Field-trip and data-collection policy already covers most relevant cases.
- **B:** Mostly. New cases that existing policy may not cover: location-data privacy when students use AI mapping tools, AI-generated demographic claims in projects, satellite imagery interpretation responsibility.
- **P:** Specific gap-filling cases for geography departments, identifying where existing policy doesn't reach AI-era practice.

### M12 × foreign_languages
- **R:** Translation-tool policy is what colleagues mean when they say "AI policy." That conversation is exhausted.
- **B:** Translation is one cell in a much larger map. Other cells: AI as conversation partner (privacy of student speech data), AI in pronunciation feedback (what counts as cheating), AI in writing support (where the line is in a second language).
- **P:** Policy cases beyond the translation-tool debate, drawn from the wider footprint AI now has in language classrooms.

### M12 × social_studies
- **R:** Policy-drafting is in my curriculum. The module risks reinventing the wheel for my specialty.
- **B:** Curriculum policy-drafting is content. Classroom AI policy-drafting is operational. Different scale and stakes — closer to a workplace policy than to a public policy.
- **P:** Operational policy practice (your classroom, your department) distinct from the public-policy curriculum content you teach.

### M12 × computer_science
- **R:** I'm asked to draft school-wide AI policy already. The expectation is that I can do this.
- **B:** Drafting school-wide AI policy is hard, and there's almost no formal preparation for it. The module is exactly the preparation that's currently missing.
- **P:** Structured preparation for the school-wide policy drafting you're likely already being asked to do, replacing the current improvise-from-first-principles approach.

### M12 × physical_education
- **R:** PE-specific AI policy doesn't exist anywhere. I'd be drafting from scratch.
- **B:** Drafting from scratch is the contribution. The module supports first-drafts in under-served subjects, where existing policy templates don't reach.
- **P:** Drafting support for PE-specific AI policy, claiming territory where general policy doesn't yet reach. Pioneering work in a small but unique area.

### M12 × arts
- **R:** Art policy on AI image generation is impossible to draft cleanly. The lines don't hold.
- **B:** They don't hold cleanly anywhere. The module's drafting approach accepts this and aims for "least-bad" policy rather than coherent policy: explicit known unknowns, planned revisions, documented trade-offs.
- **P:** A drafting approach for genuinely contested territory, suited to art classrooms where coherent AI policy may not be possible right now.

### M12 × special_education
- **R:** SEN gets specific exemptions in most school policies, which means SEN drafting is afterthought work.
- **B:** Drafting SEN-specific provisions early, rather than as exemptions added later, changes the policy entirely. The module supports SEN-leading drafts that other students' provisions get bolted onto.
- **P:** A drafting strategy where SEN provisions structure the policy rather than being attached as exceptions, with explicit moves to claim that drafting position.

### M12 × early_childhood
- **R:** Early years is rarely included in school AI policy because we don't use the AI tools in question.
- **B:** Use is widening fast. Drafting early-years-specific AI policy now, before products arrive, sets the boundaries that later staff will inherit. Pre-emptive policy.
- **P:** A pre-emptive policy drafting approach for early years, ahead of product arrival, when the drafting is easier and the resulting boundaries are stronger.

### M12 × other (Primary Generalist)
- **R:** Policy-drafting feels secondary-school. Primary policy is mostly behaviour and safeguarding.
- **B:** Primary AI policy fits inside safeguarding and behaviour policy frames. The drafting work is updating existing primary-specific policies to address AI cases, not building parallel structures.
- **P:** Primary-specific drafting that extends existing safeguarding and behaviour policy to cover AI cases, in the form your school is already used to.

### M12 × Universal
- **R:** Policy is for someone else to write. I just implement.
- **B:** "Just implement" still requires interpretation, and your interpretation becomes policy by precedent. The module surfaces this implicit drafting and makes it intentional.
- **P:** Recognition that you may already be drafting policy informally through your teaching choices, with frames for doing so explicitly.

# M13 — Multimodal AI Content Creation
## UNESCO Aspect 3, Create Level

**Module focus:** Creating content with image generation, audio, and video AI tools. Tool selection, copyright awareness, classroom decisions about disclosure. Builds on M3 and M8.

**Generic resistance pattern:** "Multimodal AI content creation is for media specialists. I'm a subject teacher." This module looks technical and creative-industries-flavoured, repelling teachers who don't see themselves as content producers.

---

### M13 × mathematics
- **R:** Maths doesn't need multimodal content. Diagrams, equations, and worked examples are all I produce.
- **B:** Diagrams are a multimodal output. AI diagram tools, animation generators, and even voice-over for video explanations are all multimodal AI applied to maths-teaching content.
- **P:** Specific multimodal AI tools for maths content (diagram generation, animated walkthroughs, audio explanations), with judgement on when each pays off.

### M13 × language_arts
- **R:** I want students writing words, not generating images. Multimodal AI is a distraction from my subject.
- **B:** Multimodal AI use as a teaching tool, in your prep, is different from student use of multimodal AI in their work. The module focuses on the first.
- **P:** Teacher-side multimodal applications (visual scaffolds for texts, audio versions for accessibility, illustrated vocabulary support) that serve word-focused learning rather than dilute it.

### M13 × science
- **R:** Generating science visuals risks producing pretty wrong things. The cost-benefit is poor.
- **B:** Right for content visuals (e.g., "draw a mitochondrion" — risky). Lower-risk applications: animated worked examples, audio explanations of your own diagrams, video summaries of practicals you've recorded.
- **P:** A risk map of multimodal AI in science teaching, with safer applications named explicitly and the risky ones quarantined.

### M13 × physics
- **R:** Visualisation of physics concepts is precise work. AI tools will produce convincing nonsense.
- **B:** Convincing nonsense is the central risk. The module's physics applications avoid concept-visualisation and focus on applications where AI produces support material around your accurate visuals — voice-overs, accessibility versions, transcript generation.
- **P:** Multimodal applications that respect physics' visualisation precision by working around your accurate diagrams rather than producing replacement ones.

### M13 × chemistry
- **R:** Chemistry visualisations need accuracy down to bond angles. AI image tools won't deliver.
- **B:** They won't, and the module accepts this. Useful applications: producing audio versions of your written explanations, animating chemical processes from your storyboards, generating video transitions between concepts.
- **P:** Multimodal applications that use your chemistry expertise as input and produce alternative formats as output, rather than asking AI to know chemistry.

### M13 × biology
- **R:** Biology image generation produces wrong cellular structure, wrong anatomy, wrong species traits. Useless.
- **B:** Image generation is a small slice. Other applications: audio descriptions of your microscopy images, video walkthroughs of dissections from your photos, narrated animations of your own diagrams.
- **P:** Biology multimodal applications that route around image generation entirely, focusing on alternative formats of accurate content you've produced.

### M13 × history
- **R:** AI-generated historical imagery is a particular ethics problem — fake faces, fake events. I don't want to encourage it.
- **B:** Historical imagery generation isn't recommended in the module. Recommended applications: audio versions of primary sources, narrated map animations, transcript generation for archival video.
- **P:** Multimodal applications that respect history's image-ethics problem by avoiding image generation entirely, with focus on audio and animation of accurate sources.

### M13 × geography
- **R:** Geographic visuals must be accurate to place. AI struggles with specific locations.
- **B:** Specific-location image generation is unreliable, but geographic teaching uses many other multimodal forms: narrated atlas animations, audio descriptions of fieldwork sites, video summaries from your fieldwork footage.
- **P:** Multimodal applications grounded in your accurate location data, using AI only for format conversion and accessibility extension.

### M13 × foreign_languages
- **R:** Multimodal AI in language teaching means video conversation partners. Privacy and quality concerns make me cautious.
- **B:** Conversation partners are one application. Lower-risk: AI-generated comprehensible-input audio at controlled levels, video transcripts for authentic materials, voice-acted versions of student-written dialogues.
- **P:** Lower-risk multimodal applications for language learning that don't require students to interact with AI directly, focusing on AI-as-production-tool for prep materials.

### M13 × social_studies
- **R:** Multimodal AI generates the deepfakes I'm trying to teach students to recognise. Using it myself feels contradictory.
- **B:** Using it intentionally and showing the workings is exactly the lesson. The teacher who creates an AI image, points out the production process, and discusses detection is teaching the literacy directly.
- **P:** A teaching pattern where your own multimodal AI use becomes content for the literacy you're teaching, instead of a contradiction to it.

### M13 × computer_science
- **R:** I know multimodal AI tools. The module won't introduce me to anything new.
- **B:** Tool knowledge is one part. The teacher-decision part — when to use, when not to, how to model decision-making for students — is less covered in CS curricula and more useful in your role.
- **P:** Decision-making frames for multimodal AI use that complement your tool knowledge, useful when modelling AI choice-making to students.

### M13 × physical_education
- **R:** Multimodal AI for PE? Beyond a few stretching videos, hard to imagine.
- **B:** PE-specific applications: voice-narrated drill demonstrations, audio descriptions for accessibility, video summaries of student performance from your footage. Modest but useful.
- **P:** A small set of PE-specific multimodal applications that fit prep-stage work, keeping class-time AI-free if you choose.

### M13 × arts
- **R:** Image generation is core to this module. I have strong reservations about it in art teaching.
- **B:** Reservations are reasonable, and the module includes them. Applications proposed: image generation as a contrast to student work (not a replacement), audio analysis of student work, video time-lapse summaries.
- **P:** An honest treatment of image generation in art teaching, with applications proposed only where the AI image is contrasted-with rather than substituted-for student work.

### M13 × special_education
- **R:** Multimodal AI tools are accessibility tools first. I should know this content already.
- **B:** Many SEN teachers do, and this module's contribution is more on the production-side: creating multimodal versions of materials you've designed (audio versions, video walkthroughs, image descriptions) at scale.
- **P:** Production-side multimodal techniques for SEN teachers who already know the consumption-side accessibility tools, focused on creating differentiated materials at scale.

### M13 × early_childhood
- **R:** Early years materials should be human-made and warm. AI multimodal feels cold.
- **B:** AI multimodal can be your prep, with the human-made warmth at delivery. Applications: AI-generated story-prompt images you reflect on with children, audio versions of your own learning stories for parent communication, video summaries of children's work for portfolios.
- **P:** Adult-side multimodal AI prep that supports the warm, human delivery early years requires, with no AI in child-facing material.

### M13 × other (Primary Generalist)
- **R:** Multimodal AI looks like a media production module. I don't have time to learn five AI tools.
- **B:** The module recommends two or three reusable tools, not five. The same image-generation tool produces visuals across your subjects; the same audio tool reads multiple texts.
- **P:** A short list of multimodal tools selected for primary-generalist reuse across subjects, not a tour of every available platform.

### M13 × Universal
- **R:** Multimodal AI sounds like content creation for marketing teams, not classroom teachers.
- **B:** The module's framing is teacher-as-prep-producer, not content-creator. Multimodal applications focus on audio/video versions of materials you already produce, not on becoming a media designer.
- **P:** Specific multimodal applications for routine teacher prep, with no expectation of becoming a content creator beyond that.

# M14 — AI-Engaged Pedagogy: Roles and Strategies
## UNESCO Aspect 4, Create Level

**Module focus:** Pedagogical roles in AI-augmented classrooms (drawing on Five Roles framework, Potkalitsky 2026), gamification, advanced lesson patterns. Builds on M4 and M9.

**Generic resistance pattern:** "I have my pedagogy. New roles and strategies feel like a re-training." This module risks being heard as "abandon what works for new approaches."

---

### M14 × mathematics
- **R:** Maths pedagogy is well-developed. Adding "AI roles" risks importing language that doesn't fit.
- **B:** The Five Roles framework is descriptive of moves you may already make: explainer, facilitator, evaluator, designer, learner. Adding "in AI contexts" doesn't change the moves; it labels them.
- **P:** Vocabulary for moves you already do, applied to AI-augmented teaching, useful for talking with colleagues about what you choose to keep human.

### M14 × language_arts
- **R:** Pedagogy modules tend to assume my classroom is more structured than it is.
- **B:** The Five Roles describe what you do in any moment, regardless of structure. They're observation tools, not lesson templates.
- **P:** Observation-tool framing for the Five Roles, useful for noticing your own moves rather than for restructuring your classroom.

### M14 × science
- **R:** Gamification and pedagogy strategies are usually fluffy. I want operational guidance.
- **B:** Gamification in this module is bounded: specific game elements (points, competition, narrative) used for specific purposes, with explicit cases of when each backfires. Operational, not motivational.
- **P:** Bounded gamification with specific use cases and explicit failure modes, suited to teachers who want operational rather than inspirational guidance.

### M14 × physics
- **R:** Physics pedagogy is conceptually careful. Game elements risk making learning superficial.
- **B:** That risk is real, and the module names it. Recommended placements: gamification of practice (drill repetition), not of concept introduction. Different cognitive territory.
- **P:** A clear placement-rule (gamify practice, not concept) that protects physics' conceptual core while allowing engagement-aiding game elements where they help.

### M14 × chemistry
- **R:** Chemistry classroom strategies are constrained by safety. Most pedagogy modules ignore this.
- **B:** The module's chemistry-specific notes include safety constraints explicitly: which strategies fit lab work, which fit theory, which require seating arrangements that lab benches won't allow.
- **P:** Pedagogical strategies that respect chemistry's classroom constraints, with explicit attention to safety-shaped seating, equipment, and group dynamics.

### M14 × biology
- **R:** Biology has a content-coverage problem. Adding new pedagogical strategies makes coverage harder.
- **B:** Coverage isn't increased by the strategies; engagement is. The module's case is that engagement-shaped strategies cover content faster, not slower, when applied selectively.
- **P:** A case for selective strategy use focused on coverage-efficiency, not on engagement-for-its-own-sake. Suited to teachers under content-pressure.

### M14 × history
- **R:** History pedagogy has rich traditions. Importing AI-era strategies risks distracting from them.
- **B:** Importing isn't replacing. The module shows how AI-era strategies extend existing history-teaching moves (source analysis, perspective-taking, narrative comparison) rather than displacing them.
- **P:** Extension-of-existing-traditions framing, with AI-era strategies plugged into history's pedagogical lineage rather than imported alongside it.

### M14 × geography
- **R:** Geography pedagogy depends on fieldwork and place. Classroom strategy modules don't reach this.
- **B:** Strategy applications include: fieldwork-prep gamification, place-based investigation roles, cross-classroom collaboration on shared spatial problems. Not desk-bound.
- **P:** Strategies that travel out of the classroom and into the field, suited to geography's place-based work.

### M14 × foreign_languages
- **R:** Communicative language teaching has its own well-developed pedagogy. New strategy modules muddy it.
- **B:** The module's CLT-compatibility section is explicit. Strategies that fit communicative aims are included; strategies that pull toward translation-drilling are flagged as anti-CLT.
- **P:** Explicit CLT-compatibility analysis for proposed strategies, so you can adopt selectively without disrupting the communicative orientation you've built.

### M14 × social_studies
- **R:** Social studies pedagogy is contested between content-focused and skills-focused traditions. New strategies often pick a side.
- **B:** The module's strategies are designed to serve either tradition, with explicit notes on how they look in each. You don't have to take a side to use them.
- **P:** Tradition-neutral strategy implementations, with notes on how each looks within content-focused vs skills-focused practice.

### M14 × computer_science
- **R:** I gamify naturally — much of CS teaching is project-based already. The module may be repetitive.
- **B:** CS-specific contributions: roles for AI-pair-programming sessions, ethical dilemma scenarios as assessment, code-review-with-AI as a specific pedagogical pattern.
- **P:** CS-specific strategy patterns that go beyond the project-based work you may already use, focused on the AI-collaboration dynamics increasingly common in your subject.

### M14 × physical_education
- **R:** PE is gamified by definition — sport is play. Adding gamification frameworks feels redundant.
- **B:** Sport is one form of gamification; the module covers the other (extrinsic motivation, points/rewards, narrative framing) with explicit notes on PE-specific risks (over-extrinsic motivation displacing intrinsic enjoyment of movement).
- **P:** PE-specific notes on gamification's risks (motivation displacement, competitive harm) alongside its uses, suited to a subject already deeply gamified in its core practice.

### M14 × arts
- **R:** Art teaching values process and presence. Strategy frameworks lean toward product-and-output thinking.
- **B:** Module's arts-specific notes include process-protected strategies: gamification of skill drills (not of art-making), role-rotation for collaborative work (not for solo making), AI-engagement at critique stage (not at making stage).
- **P:** Strategy patterns explicitly arranged to protect process-and-presence, with AI-engagement quarantined to critique and skill-development moments.

### M14 × special_education

- **B:** Frameworks in the module are presented as menus of options, not as standards. SEN-specific notes include explicit individualisation moves and warnings about over-application.

# M15 — Professional Transformation and Research Leadership
## UNESCO Aspect 5, Create Level

**Module focus:** Capstone-level professional transformation. Optional action research, sharing practice, joining wider professional community. Builds on M5 and M10.

**Generic resistance pattern:** "Research leadership is for academics. I'm a teacher." The capstone module faces the strongest imposter response in the platform, because the language of research and leadership feels institutional.

### M15 × mathematics
- **R:** Maths-teaching research is dominated by university researchers. My contribution would be ignored.
- **B:** Classroom-research traditions in maths are well-established (Lesson Study, Action Research). Most aren't published academically and don't aim to be. They circulate among practitioners and inform practice.
- **P:** A practitioner-research framing distinct from academic publication, with examples of how maths-teaching research moves through practitioner networks regardless of academic recognition.

### M15 × language_arts
- **R:** I write professionally elsewhere. Research writing is a different genre I haven't trained in.
- **B:** Practitioner research writing is closer to professional reflection essays than to academic articles. Existing strong writing transfers more directly than the research-writing label suggests.
- **P:** Genre clarification — practitioner research is a transferable extension of professional reflection writing — useful for teachers who already write but feel research-writing is alien.

### M15 × science
- **R:** Action research in education isn't real science. The methods are loose.
- **B:** True for educational research generally, and the module accepts it. The framing isn't "this is rigorous" but "this is structured noticing." A small step up from informal teacher reflection, not a small step down from controlled experiment.
- **P:** An honest framing of action research as structured noticing, neither inflated nor dismissed, suited to science teachers who'd see through false rigour-claims.

### M15 × physics
- **R:** Physics teachers are particularly likely to call out methodological weakness in education research. I'd rather not produce something I'd criticise myself.
- **B:** The module's research patterns emphasise observation over claim. The action-research output documents what you tried and what you noticed; it doesn't claim cause-effect or generalisability.
- **P:** Research patterns suited to physics teachers' methodological scepticism, focused on observation-documentation rather than causal claims.

### M15 × chemistry
- **R:** I have no research training. This module assumes capacities I don't have.
- **B:** No formal research training is needed. The capacities required are noticing, recording, reflecting — which you already deploy in safety supervision and lab observation. New domain, same skills.
- **P:** A no-formal-training framing that draws on capacities you already use in classroom supervision, redirected into structured AI noticing.

### M15 × biology
- **R:** Practitioner research is a genre I don't read. I wouldn't know what good looks like.
- **B:** Module includes practitioner-research examples specifically chosen for accessibility — short, structured, modest in claim. Looking like good is closer to your written reflections than to academic articles.
- **P:** Examples of practitioner research at the level you might produce, demystifying the genre.

### M15 × history
- **R:** History teachers research history; we're not researchers of teaching.
- **B:** Researching teaching uses many of the same skills as researching history: contextualisation, source-evaluation, narrative-construction. The historian-as-teacher-researcher is a natural transfer.
- **P:** A skill-transfer framing for history teachers, with action research as a redirection of historical-research skills toward classroom phenomena.

### M15 × geography
- **R:** Field-research skills don't translate cleanly to classroom research.
- **B:** They translate more than expected: observation protocols, sampling questions, contextualisation, write-up under length constraints. Field-research literacy is closer to classroom-research literacy than either is to laboratory science.
- **P:** A transfer-framing for geographic field-research literacy, applied to classroom AI-noticing.

### M15 × computer_science

- **B:** Most CS-education research is on undergraduates and on coding-specific learning. K-12 CS-with-AI is largely under-researched, with practitioners better positioned to observe than university researchers.
- **P:** A specifically under-researched territory (K-12 CS in AI era) where practitioner observation has unusual value, partially addressing the "won't add" concern.

### M15 × physical_education

### M15 × arts

- **B:** Rare and emerging. The module includes art-specific examples: action research on AI in critique, longitudinal observation of student creative confidence, case studies of AI-resistant studio cultures.
- **P:** Art-teaching-specific research examples in an emerging space, useful as starting points for your own work.

### M15 × special_education

- **B:** Practitioner research in SEN is one of the more accepted forms because controlled studies are often ethically and practically impossible. Single-case practitioner research has standing in SEN literature.

### M15 × early_childhood

- **B:** Early-years-specific practitioner research is well-established (learning stories as research artefacts, observation-narrative-reflection cycles). The module's framing fits early years more than secondary, in fact.
- **P:** Early-years-fit research patterns, drawn from learning-story traditions, redirected toward AI-noticing in early-years contexts.

### M15 × other (Primary Generalist)

- **B:** Small-scope, in-house dissemination is exactly the form practitioner research takes. The module's leadership framing is informal: shaping what colleagues consider plausible, through documented examples from your own practice.
- **P:** A small-scope, low-status framing of leadership that fits primary-generalist reality, distinct from the institutional-leadership language elsewhere in the module.

### M15 × Universal

- **B:** This module's outputs are scaled to what's possible: a short structured reflection, a colleague conversation with notes, a one-page case study. Floor outputs, not ceiling outputs.
- **P:** Floor-level capstone outputs explicitly designed for completion rather than for impressive deliverable production.

### What's distinctive about Create level

The voice across Create-level cells is **smaller, more permission-giving, more floor-than-ceiling**. The Acquire level argued relevance ("this is for you"). The Deepen level reduced friction ("this is doable"). The Create level lowers the bar for action ("this isn't as big as it sounds").

Specific moves recurring across this batch:

- **"Leadership without status"** (M11) — informal influence reframed as leadership, no positional authority required
- **"Drafting from below"** (M12) — policy work without management endorsement
- **"Multimodal as prep, not production"** (M13) — content creation reframed as teacher prep, not media work
- **"Strategies as menu, not curriculum"** (M14) — strategy frameworks as observation tools or selectable patterns, not as new pedagogies to adopt
- **"Practitioner research, not academic"** (M15) — research framed as structured noticing, not as publication-bound work

### Notable resistance patterns in Create level

The dominant resistance is **identity mismatch**: "I'm not a leader / not a researcher / not a media person / not a policy expert." Bridges across the batch don't argue against this directly. They reframe the action so that the identity claim no longer applies:

- "Lead" → "shape conversations you're already in"
- "Research" → "document what you already noticed"
- "Create" → "prep what you'd have prepped anyway, in different formats"
- "Draft policy" → "extend rules you already have"

This pattern is consistent enough that it could be reported as a Phase 3 design finding in the dissertation: **resistance reduction at Create level requires action-reframing rather than identity-affirmation**.
