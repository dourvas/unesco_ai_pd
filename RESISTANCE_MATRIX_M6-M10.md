**Previous batch:** M1–M5 (Acquire) — approved
**Next batch:** M11–M15 (Create)

---

## How to read this document

Each cell answers three questions for a (subject × module) pair:

- **R** (Resistance) — what this teacher might be silently thinking when they see this module
- **B** (Bridge) — the classroom-recognisable connection that makes the module their territory
- **P** (Promise) — the specific gain this teacher takes from this module

The 17 cells per module are: 16 subjects + 1 Universal fallback.

---

## What changes at the Deepen level

The Acquire-level resistance was "this module isn't for me." The Deepen-level resistance shifts to "I get the basics, but the next step looks complicated or impractical." Teachers at this stage have absorbed the Acquire content and now meet sophistication.

Resistance becomes more focused, less philosophical:
- "I see what AI can do; I'm not sure I want to."
- "This is interesting in theory; my Tuesday afternoon needs something simpler."
- "I'm being asked to commit to something I don't yet trust."

Bridges therefore lean on continuity from the Acquire module in the same strand, not on first principles. Promises get more specific because the teacher is closer to action.

# M6 — Human-Centred AI: Identity and Values
## UNESCO Aspect 1, Deepen Level

**Module focus:** What stays human in AI-augmented teaching? Identity, professional values, the irreducible human role. Builds on M1's introduction.

**Generic resistance pattern:** "Identity discussions are abstract. I want practical content." Teachers at the Deepen level often want tools, not introspection. This module is honest that the identity work is itself the tool.

---

### M6 × mathematics
- **R:** Identity questions are for humanities people. Mathematics is content-driven.
- **B:** Mathematics teaching is full of identity work: who's "a maths person", who isn't, who gets called intuitive vs procedural. AI surfaces these labels in new ways — through recommendation systems, automated grading, learning analytics.
- **P:** A frame for noticing where AI tools are quietly making identity judgements about your students, before those judgements harden into tracking decisions.

### M6 × language_arts
- **R:** AI threatens what I do. Talking about identity won't change the threat.
- **B:** The threat is real, and the identity question is exactly what helps you respond. What part of writing instruction is irreducibly yours? Once you can name it, you can defend it from automation.
- **P:** Language to articulate what you bring that AI doesn't, useful for parent meetings, department conversations, and your own confidence on hard days.

### M6 × science
- **R:** Science teaching is about evidence and method. Identity is sociology.
- **B:** Method is identity, in disguise. The scientist's stance — sceptical, evidence-based, transparent about uncertainty — is what separates a science teacher from an AI delivering science facts.
- **P:** A way to teach the scientific stance, not just the scientific content, with AI as a useful contrast that makes the stance visible.

### M6 × physics
- **R:** Physics is the same whether a human or AI explains it. Identity isn't part of the equation.
- **B:** It is, in two places: how you choose what to teach (curriculum is values), and how you teach failure productively (AI confidently fails physics, you don't). Both are deeply human moves.
- **P:** Recognition of where physics teaching depends on you specifically — your selection, your handling of uncertainty — that remains irreducibly human.

### M6 × chemistry
- **R:** Chemistry is dangerous when wrong. Identity questions feel like distraction.
- **B:** Safety culture is identity. The teacher who builds careful chemistry practice in students is teaching a way of being, not just a list of rules. AI doesn't transmit ethos; it transmits content.
- **P:** A frame for understanding chemistry teaching as ethos transmission, which protects what you do from being seen as content-delivery that AI could replace.

### M6 × biology
- **R:** Identity is psychology, not biology. I teach the biology of organisms, not professional self-reflection.
- **B:** Biology teachers shape how students view their own bodies, their place in nature, their species' future. Identity is unavoidable in biology classrooms; AI just pushes the question into the open.
- **P:** Tools for handling identity-laden topics in biology — body image, gender, race in genetics, environmental ethics — alongside AI's increasing presence in those discussions.

### M6 × history
- **R:** Historians teach about identity all the time. Probably old territory for me.
- **B:** Likely true for student identity in historical context. Less covered: your own identity as a teacher in the AI moment. Are you a curator? An interpreter? A guide through contested narratives? AI forces the question.
- **P:** Direct engagement with your own identity as a history teacher in the AI era — useful for departmental conversations and for your own choice of stance.

### M6 × geography
- **R:** Geography is about places and people, not professional self-image.
- **B:** Geography teachers regularly help students locate themselves: in a city, a region, a global system. The same skills apply to locating yourself as a teacher in the AI ecosystem — what's local, what's global, what's automated, what's irreplaceable.
- **P:** A geographically inflected identity exercise, drawing on your existing skill of helping students situate themselves spatially and culturally.

### M6 × foreign_languages
- **R:** Translation tools keep getting better. My identity as a language teacher feels under siege.
- **B:** Under siege is exactly the right description, which is why this module exists. The path through is articulating what you do that translation tools don't: relationship, motivation, error-as-learning, cultural transmission. None of that is automated.
- **P:** A defended position on your professional value, drawn from explicit features of language teaching that AI doesn't replicate. Useful for your own resilience.

### M6 × social_studies
- **R:** Identity is core to my curriculum. This module sounds like content I already deliver.
- **B:** You deliver student identity work skilfully. Less likely is that you've systematically considered your own — as a social studies teacher whose subject is being reshaped by AI surveillance, AI bias, AI-generated political content. Your students will need your stance.
- **P:** Time to articulate your own positioning on AI in your subject area, before students push the question and you respond from improvisation.

### M6 × computer_science
- **R:** As a CS teacher, my professional identity is closer to the AI than to the humanities teachers. This module isn't really for me.
- **B:** It is, with a twist. CS teachers are increasingly asked to be the ethical voice in their school's AI conversations — a role most weren't prepared for. Identity-as-CS-teacher needs renegotiation as the field shifts.
- **P:** A framework for handling the new ethical-spokesperson role that CS teachers find themselves in, distinct from the technical role.

### M6 × physical_education
- **R:** PE is bodies, sport, presence. Identity in AI era? Hard to see the connection.
- **B:** PE is one of the few subjects where AI struggles to replace anything central. Movement coaching, team dynamics, body confidence — all human. That position is rare and worth understanding.
- **P:** Recognition of PE's particular AI-resilience, useful for your own confidence and for advocating for the subject's value when curriculum decisions get made.

### M6 × arts
- **R:** Art is about human expression. AI's encroachment is exactly what I'm trying to resist.
- **B:** Resistance is one stance; the module helps you articulate it as a defended position, not a default reaction. What about your art teaching is irreducibly human, and how do you communicate that to students who'll be told otherwise online?
- **P:** A clear, articulable position on human creativity in your classroom, robust to the inevitable "but ChatGPT can do that" challenge.

### M6 × special_education
- **R:** My identity as an SEN teacher is already clear: advocate, individualiser, careful presence. I don't need a module on it.
- **B:** Probably not. But AI tools will increasingly be sold as solutions for your students. Your identity as the irreplaceable individualiser is exactly what protects students from over-automated provision.
- **P:** Reinforcement of professional ground you already stand on, with vocabulary for resisting automation pressure that will only grow.

### M6 × early_childhood
- **R:** Early years is human-centred by definition. This module is preaching to the choir.
- **B:** Possibly, but worth checking. Even in early years, AI products are expanding: voice assistants in classrooms, automated literacy tracking, "personalised" learning apps for three-year-olds. Your human-centred identity gets tested in concrete decisions.
- **P:** Concrete criteria for testing AI-product proposals against your human-centred stance, rather than relying on instinct alone.

### M6 × other (Primary Generalist)
- **R:** I'm the relationship adult for 25 children all day. My identity is not in question; my time is.
- **B:** Your identity is exactly what's at stake when AI tools are pitched as efficiency aids that "free time for relationship building." What if the time saved by AI is the time of relationship building, in disguise?
- **P:** A clear-eyed look at AI efficiency claims in primary settings, with attention to whether the "saved" time is in fact relational time being relocated, not freed.

### M6 × Universal
- **R:** Identity work feels like a soft module without practical output.
- **B:** This module's output is your stance: what you defend, what you concede, what you delegate. Without a stance, AI integration decisions get made by inertia and vendor pressure.
- **P:** A defended stance on your role in AI-augmented teaching, useful for any decision involving AI in your classroom from now on.

# M7 — Applied AI Ethics in Classroom Practice
## UNESCO Aspect 2, Deepen Level

**Module focus:** Moving from ethical principles to classroom decisions. Case studies, dilemmas, classroom rules, dealing with student misuse. Builds on M2's foundations.

**Generic resistance pattern:** "I know the principles. I need to know what to actually do on Monday." Deepen-level ethics frustration is common because principles feel abstract until they meet specific situations.

---

### M7 × mathematics
- **R:** Maths is unaffected by ethics in daily lessons. Where do I apply this?
- **B:** Daily applications: deciding whether to allow AI for homework, handling the student who used it without saying, choosing whether to use AI-generated problem sets you can't fully verify. All concrete, all this week.
- **P:** Classroom decision rules for the specific AI ethics moments that arise in maths teaching, ready for use without further translation.

### M7 × language_arts
- **R:** This module will be all about AI-written essays. I've heard it.
- **B:** The essay question is in here, but it's one of many. Also covered: AI as a feedback partner, voice authenticity in AI-assisted drafts, student peer review when some peers use AI, parents asking why you "can't just check with ChatGPT."
- **P:** A wider repertoire of classroom ethics decisions in writing instruction, beyond the single essay-detection conversation that dominates the field.

### M7 × science
- **R:** Science ethics is in the curriculum. This module risks being repetitive.
- **B:** Curriculum ethics is general (animal testing, climate). Classroom ethics is specific (which AI claims do you check, how do you handle a student who AI-generated a lab report). Different territory.
- **P:** Classroom-specific ethical decisions in science teaching, distinct from the curricular ethics already covered.

### M7 × physics
- **R:** Physics has unambiguous answers. Ethics needs ambiguous problems. Strange fit.
- **B:** Classroom physics has plenty of ambiguity: which student work to trust, how to handle a confident-but-wrong AI explanation a student presents, when to allow AI for problem-checking. Operational, not theoretical.
- **P:** Decision rules for the operational ethics moments in physics teaching, where the answer isn't predetermined.

### M7 × chemistry
- **R:** I worry about students using AI to generate dangerous-knowledge essays. What's the protocol?
- **B:** Exactly the kind of case the module covers, alongside related concerns: AI giving wrong safety information, students fact-checking your safety teaching against ChatGPT, how to handle "but AI says it's fine" moments.
- **P:** A protocol for the chemistry-specific AI safety moments that the general module wouldn't anticipate.

### M7 × biology
- **R:** I want concrete classroom rules, not more principle-talk.
- **B:** This module is rules. When to allow, when to forbid, what to do when a student crosses the line, how to handle the student who only crossed because they didn't understand the line.
- **P:** Classroom rules for AI in biology learning, drafted as actual rules and not as ethical considerations.

### M7 × history
- **R:** History ethics is rich and old. New AI cases will fit existing frameworks.
- **B:** Mostly true. The specifically new cases: AI-generated primary sources presented as real, students using AI to "interview" historical figures, AI-fabricated quotes in essays. Worth handling explicitly.
- **P:** History-specific cases that don't quite fit existing frameworks, with proposed handling drawn from your existing ethical literacy.

### M7 × geography
- **R:** Geographic ethics is mostly about field ethics and respect for places. Less obvious AI angle.
- **B:** AI angle: location data privacy in fieldwork, AI-generated maps presented as accurate, automated demographic analysis that reproduces stereotypes. All operational classroom issues.
- **P:** Classroom rules for geographic AI work, focused on fieldwork prep, mapping projects, and analysis using AI tools.

### M7 × foreign_languages
- **R:** "Translation cheating" will dominate this module. I've already settled my position.
- **B:** Cheating is one cell. Other cells: AI as a pronunciation coach (when it's accurate, when it isn't), AI's cultural-flattening tendency in language production, students using AI to "check" your corrections.
- **P:** Wider ground beyond the cheating debate, including useful AI uses you may not yet have policy for.

### M7 × social_studies
- **R:** Social studies has the strongest ethical curriculum. This module risks duplicating.
- **B:** Curricular ethics covers what students study. Teacher ethics covers what you do — your AI use, your tool selection, your data sharing with vendors. Different layer.
- **P:** Teacher-side ethical practice in your subject, complementary to (not replacing) the strong curricular ethics you already deliver.

### M7 × computer_science
- **R:** I teach AI ethics. I might be teaching this module's content already.
- **B:** Possibly. The module's specific contribution is the teacher-as-decision-maker frame: when you decide to deploy AI in your CS classroom, what factors weigh?
- **P:** A teacher-decision framework distinct from the developer-decision framework you likely already teach, useful for your own pedagogical AI choices.

### M7 × physical_education
- **R:** PE rarely has classroom AI ethics moments. We're outside, mostly moving.
- **B:** AI is moving outside too: fitness apps logging student data, video analysis tools, body composition scanners. Each is an ethics decision before it's a pedagogical one.
- **P:** Decision rules for the AI products that vendors increasingly target at PE departments, before your school adopts them by default.

### M7 × arts
- **R:** Image-generation ethics is the obvious topic. Will the module say anything new?
- **B:** Image generation is one cell among several. Others: AI critique of student work (whose values?), AI-generated reference images masquerading as real artists, students "improving" peer work with AI between feedback rounds.
- **P:** A wider ethics map for art classroom AI, beyond the well-trodden image-generation debate.

### M7 × special_education
- **R:** SEN students are most affected by AI ethics failures. I want practice tools, not principles.
- **B:** Tools are what's here: how to evaluate AI accessibility tools before adopting, how to handle data-collection consent for students with limited capacity to consent, what to do when an AI tool works for most students but fails for one.
- **P:** Operational practice tools for the AI ethics situations specific to special education, where the stakes are higher than the general module conveys.

### M7 × early_childhood
- **R:** Ethics in early years is foundational and constant. Adding AI is unnecessary complication.
- **B:** AI products targeting under-fives are a fast-growing market. Your ethics work is already foundational, but it needs new vocabulary to evaluate these products specifically.
- **P:** Early-years-specific evaluation tools for AI products marketed to your setting, building on your existing ethics practice.

### M7 × other (Primary Generalist)
- **R:** I make 50 small ethics decisions every day across five subjects. I can't add an AI ethics layer to every one.
- **B:** You won't. The module gives you a small set of cross-subject decision rules that apply to AI ethics, so the layer is one rule applied many times, not many rules.
- **P:** A short, reusable set of AI ethics decision rules for primary classrooms, designed to integrate into your existing ethics-handling rather than parallel it.

### M7 × Universal
- **R:** Applied ethics modules tend to be case studies that don't quite match my situation.
- **B:** Cases here are categorised by decision type, not by surface details, so the underlying logic transfers across contexts.
- **P:** Decision rules organised by type, reusable across the specific ethics situations you'll face that this module won't have predicted.

# M8 — Reflective Prompt Engineering: Deepening Practice
## UNESCO Aspect 3, Deepen Level

**Module focus:** Hands-on prompt engineering with EduPrompt Studio. Iteration, refinement, the seven strategies in practice. Builds on M5.

**Generic resistance pattern:** "I tried prompting. It's not magic. Why a deeper module?" Teachers at the Deepen level have hit prompting's frustrations and may be ready to give up.

---

### M8 × mathematics
- **R:** I've prompted AI for maths help. It gets things wrong, sometimes confidently. Deeper practice won't fix the underlying problem.
- **B:** Right; deeper prompting doesn't make AI a mathematician. It makes you a better diagnostician of when the AI's mathematical output is useful and when it's not — and lets you produce reliable math-classroom material consistently.
- **P:** A diagnostic eye for AI maths output, plus a workflow for producing reliable problem variations and explanations through controlled prompting.

### M8 × language_arts
- **R:** As a writer, I should be good at prompting. If my prompts aren't producing what I want, the tool's at fault.
- **B:** Sometimes. More often, prompting is a different genre with conventions you haven't yet learned — closer to drafting a brief for a difficult intern than to writing prose. The seven strategies make those conventions explicit.
- **P:** Genre-specific writing skills for prompts, drawing on your existing writing strength but adding the AI-specific moves you may have been missing.

### M8 × science
- **R:** Effective prompting must be experimental. I don't have time for systematic experiments on every lesson prep task.
- **B:** Hands-on prompting in this module isn't open-ended experimentation; it's structured iteration with feedback at each step. Closer to a focused inquiry than to research design.
- **P:** A small, structured iteration workflow that produces working prompts in minutes, not the open-ended experiments you reasonably want to avoid.

### M8 × physics
- **R:** I tried complex prompts. The AI lost the thread halfway through. Deeper prompting probably hits the same wall.
- **B:** It does, and the module names the wall: context windows, attention attenuation, drift over long generations. Deeper prompting is partly about not building prompts that the wall will defeat.
- **P:** Awareness of where the AI's limits actually live, so you build prompts that work within them rather than struggling against them.

### M8 × chemistry
- **R:** I want repeatable prompts. AI's variability defeats reuse.
- **B:** Variability is reduced by tighter context-setting — one of the seven strategies — and by lower temperature settings where the tool allows. Both covered hands-on.
- **P:** Specific moves to reduce AI variability where it matters in chemistry teaching, with the trade-offs (less creative output) made explicit.

### M8 × biology
- **R:** Detailed biology prompts produce confident wrong answers. The pattern is consistent.
- **B:** Consistent with what AI is built for: producing plausible-sounding text. The module's deeper practice teaches you to constrain the output to verification-friendly forms (cite-or-decline, structured) rather than free generation.
- **P:** Output constraints that route AI toward forms you can verify quickly, reducing the confident-wrong-answer problem in your prep work.

### M8 × history
- **R:** AI hallucinates dates, names, sources. No prompting strategy fixes that.
- **B:** True for content generation. The module's history applications focus on prompts that work on text you provide (analysis, comparison, differentiation) rather than prompts that ask AI to recall.
- **P:** A division of prompt types — generation vs analysis-on-source — with classroom uses in each, grounded in what AI can actually do reliably.

### M8 × geography
- **R:** Geography prompts hit AI's localism gap fast. Hard to make them useful.
- **B:** Localism is one limit; others matter too. The module's deeper practice teaches you to prompt for transferable structures (e.g., "ways to approach studying any river system") rather than local content (e.g., "tell me about the Aliakmonas").
- **P:** Geographic prompting that works across locations because it asks for analytical structures, not local facts AI may not know.

### M8 × foreign_languages
- **R:** Multilingual prompting introduces extra failure modes. Probably more frustration than payoff.
- **B:** Multilingual prompting is genuinely harder. The module addresses it: prompting in the target language vs prompting about the target language, when each works, and how to handle the unreliability gracefully.
- **P:** Specific moves for prompting in multilingual contexts, with realistic expectations about which use cases pay off and which don't.

### M8 × social_studies
- **R:** Prompting's appearance of neutrality hides political choices. Deepening the practice deepens that problem.
- **B:** Right, and the module engages with it. The seven strategies' framing as reflective practice surfaces value-laden choices (audience, format, framing) rather than hiding them.
- **P:** Awareness of which prompting moves carry political weight, useful for your subject and for your own resistance to seemingly neutral AI workflows.

### M8 × computer_science
- **R:** I know prompting techniques. EduPrompt Studio looks like a wrapper.
- **B:** It's a wrapper, but with a specific function: enforcing the seven strategies as a habit-formation tool for non-CS teachers. Worth looking at as a teaching artefact, regardless of whether you'd use it personally.
- **P:** A view of EduPrompt Studio as a pedagogy-of-prompting tool, useful when you're asked to support colleagues' AI work in your school.

### M8 × physical_education
- **R:** Hands-on prompting practice is desk-bound. PE is the wrong context.
- **B:** Prep is desk-bound. The deeper practice produces better warm-up variations, drill rotations, parent communications about sport injuries. Quick-payback at the desk.
- **P:** Specific PE-prep prompting recipes that pay off in saved time at the desk, leaving more time for the field.

### M8 × arts
- **R:** Image generation prompting must dominate this. I'd rather not.
- **B:** This module is text-prompting. Image generation is M13. Text-prompting for art teaching: writing critique scaffolds, drafting artist research questions, generating gallery-tour discussion prompts.
- **P:** Text-only prompting practice for art classrooms, leaving the image-generation question entirely separate.

### M8 × special_education
- **R:** Generic prompt examples don't translate to my students. Deepening practice probably means deeper generic.
- **B:** The module's deepen practice includes constraint-heavy prompting — exactly what SEN-specific work needs. You define audience precisely, set cognitive load constraints, specify accessibility requirements explicitly.
- **P:** Constraint-heavy prompting techniques tuned to specific SEN needs, replacing the generic outputs of casual prompting.

### M8 × early_childhood
- **R:** Prompts produce text designed for older audiences. Adapting it for under-fives is more work than writing from scratch.
- **B:** Adapting for under-fives is exactly what the audience-specification strategy makes possible. With explicit audience constraints (e.g., "language a 4-year-old uses, no abstract nouns, no metaphor"), the output becomes useable.
- **P:** A small set of early-years-specific prompt templates that produce content at the right developmental level, instead of needing rewrite from scratch.

### M8 × other (Primary Generalist)
- **R:** I tried prompting. Half the time the output needed more editing than writing from scratch would have. I'm done.
- **B:** "More editing than writing" is usually a sign of weak context-setting in the prompt. The deeper practice fixes this — by front-loading subject, age, and purpose, the output lands closer to usable on first attempt.
- **P:** Prompting moves that reduce edit time below write-time, which is the only standard that matters for a teacher with five subjects to plan.

### M8 × Universal
- **R:** Prompting practice modules tend to be theoretical or to cover only easy cases.
- **B:** This module is hands-on with hard cases. EduPrompt Studio enforces the seven strategies, surfacing what your prompts are missing.
- **P:** Hands-on practice with structured feedback, producing prompts you can keep and adapt for your subject.

# M9 — AI-Augmented Lesson Design
## UNESCO Aspect 4, Deepen Level

**Module focus:** Designing whole lessons with thoughtful AI integration. Backward design, AI placement decisions, formative checkpoints. Builds on M4.

**Generic resistance pattern:** "I design lessons fine without AI. Why complicate it?" The Deepen-level pedagogy module faces the strongest "if it isn't broken, don't fix it" reaction.

---

### M9 × mathematics
- **R:** Maths lesson design is structured. AI integration risks introducing chaos.
- **B:** AI placement in maths lessons is narrow and predictable: warm-up problem variation, worked-example generation for differentiation, exit-ticket distractor production. Three or four well-placed roles, not pervasive integration.
- **P:** A small set of evidence-based AI placements in maths lessons, with explicit guidance on where AI doesn't help (concept introduction, worked-example modelling).

### M9 × language_arts
- **R:** Lesson design with AI either makes AI central or treats it as ornament. Neither matches my practice.
- **B:** A third option: AI as a contrast tool. Student-AI versus student-only writing, used to surface what voice means. Not central, not ornament — pedagogical.
- **P:** A specific lesson pattern (AI-as-contrast) for writing instruction, designed for the 50-minute class rather than the unit-long project.

### M9 × science
- **R:** Science lessons follow predict-observe-explain cycles. AI doesn't fit cleanly.
- **B:** AI fits at the predict step (generating diverse student-style predictions for class compare), at the differentiation of the explain step, and at the formative-check stage. Not at the observe step, which stays purely empirical.
- **P:** Three specific placement points where AI strengthens predict-observe-explain cycles, with the empirical heart of science teaching protected.

### M9 × physics
- **R:** Physics lessons depend on careful conceptual sequencing. AI won't get the order right.
- **B:** AI doesn't choose order; you do. AI generates the variations you've decided to need (e.g., context variations for the same concept). Order stays in your hands.
- **P:** A workflow where AI handles the surface variation that you've designed for, leaving your conceptual sequence intact.

### M9 × chemistry
- **R:** Lesson design including AI feels like adding a dependency. What if the tool changes?
- **B:** Design the lesson to work without the AI, with AI as a quality-of-life add. Then if the tool changes, the lesson still works at the level it would have without AI.
- **P:** A design principle (AI-as-add, not AI-as-dependency) that protects your lessons against tool churn while still benefiting from current AI.

### M9 × biology
- **R:** Biology has detail-rich lessons. AI introduces detail-error risk.
- **B:** AI placement that avoids detail-generation: differentiating reading levels of texts you've vetted, drafting student-friendly explanations of figures you've made, generating quiz distractors from your own taught misconceptions.
- **P:** Biology-specific AI placements that route around the detail-error risk by working on your verified content rather than generating biology.

### M9 × history
- **R:** History lessons depend on source selection and interpretive framing. AI doesn't do either well.
- **B:** Source selection and framing remain yours. AI handles: differentiated reading-level versions of selected sources, comparison-grid generation, debate-prompt drafting. Mechanical work, not interpretive work.
- **P:** A clear separation of mechanical from interpretive work in history lesson design, with AI assigned only the mechanical.

### M9 × geography
- **R:** Geography lessons increasingly depend on current data. AI's training data is old.
- **B:** Don't use AI for current data. Use it for: generating fieldwork-prep questions, drafting differentiated atlas-reading scaffolds, producing case-study comparison frameworks. All non-temporal work.
- **P:** Geography lesson designs that quarantine AI to the non-temporal scaffolding work, leaving current data to your usual sources.

### M9 × foreign_languages
- **R:** Language lessons have communicative goals. AI integration risks turning lessons into translation exercises.
- **B:** Right, and the module addresses it. Communicative-goal lessons use AI as a conversation partner (with caveats), as a comprehensible-input generator, as a pronunciation-mirror. Not as a translation oracle.
- **P:** Lesson design moves that preserve communicative aims while using AI strategically, instead of letting AI flatten lessons into translation drills.

### M9 × social_studies
- **R:** Social studies lessons need careful framing. AI may introduce inappropriate framings.
- **B:** Likely. The module's social studies guidance includes prompt-design moves that constrain AI outputs to be evidence-based, multi-perspectival, and framing-light. Not bias-free, but workably bounded.
- **P:** Specific prompt-design moves for social studies lesson preparation that reduce (not eliminate) AI's framing-injection risk.

### M9 × computer_science
- **R:** AI-augmented lesson design in CS is well-trodden territory. Will this say anything new?
- **B:** Probably not on technical placement. The contribution is the teacher-as-pedagogical-designer framing: not "where can AI go" but "what design move does AI replace, and is that worth the trade?"
- **P:** A pedagogical-design lens that goes beyond "AI is good for X" to "AI replaces design move Y, and the trade-off is Z." Useful even for CS teachers familiar with the technical side.

### M9 × physical_education
- **R:** Lesson design for movement-based lessons doesn't involve AI in the room.
- **B:** AI in PE lesson design lives in the prep stage: drafting differentiated rule explanations, producing risk assessments, generating pre-lesson video summaries. Class-time stays AI-free if you choose.
- **P:** Prep-stage AI integration in PE that respects your class-time AI-free choice, with concrete prep-stage time savings.

### M9 × arts
- **R:** Art lessons need to protect creative space. AI lesson design risks reducing that space.
- **B:** Reducing or expanding, depending on placement. Module guidance protects making-time as AI-free; AI sits at preparation, criticism, or contrast — never at the making moment itself.
- **P:** Art lesson design rules that explicitly protect making-time from AI intrusion, with AI placed where it doesn't compete with student creation.

### M9 × special_education
- **R:** Lesson design for SEN students is highly individualised. Generic AI integration won't work.
- **B:** Individualisation is exactly where AI helps most: producing differentiated versions of materials you've designed, generating visual schedule variants, drafting communication adapted to specific student needs.
- **P:** AI integration tuned to individualisation, where the AI multiplies your personalisation work rather than averaging it.

### M9 × early_childhood
- **R:** Early years lesson design follows children's interests. AI prep is a poor match for emergent practice.
- **B:** AI prep for emergent practice is light and reactive: morning-after drafting of learning stories from observation notes, follow-up activity ideas based on observed interest, parent-communication drafting from your bullet points.
- **P:** Light, reactive AI prep that supports emergent practice without imposing pre-planned structure on it.

The voice across cells in this batch is calmer than M1–M5. Acquire-level resistance was loud ("this isn't for me"). Deepen-level resistance is quieter and more pragmatic ("I tried, I'm not sure it's worth more"). The bridges accept fatigue as a starting point rather than dismissing it.
