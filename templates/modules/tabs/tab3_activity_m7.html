AI detection tools are widely used — and widely misunderstood. Before you can make an ethical decision about whether to use them, you need to understand what they actually do and what they cannot do.

Read three short writing samples and identify which you believe was AI-generated. Then you'll encounter the research — and build your personal protocol.

Step 1 — Read these three writing samples. Which one do you think was written by AI?

Sample A

Key considerations include the potential for personalised learning pathways, the importance of maintaining academic integrity, and the need for teachers to develop appropriate digital literacy skills. Ultimately, a balanced approach that leverages AI's capabilities while preserving the centrality of human judgment appears most productive."

Sample B

"I wasn't sure I wanted to write about this topic at first. It felt too abstract — 'AI in education' sounds like something from a conference brochure. But then I thought about my student last year who stayed after class to show me a conversation he'd had with ChatGPT about the French Revolution. He was more engaged with that conversation than he'd been with anything I'd taught him all term. That made me uncomfortable. Still does."

Sample C

"AI tools in classrooms remind me of calculators in math class — everyone was worried they'd make students lazy, but we figured out how to use them well. The difference is calculators don't pretend to be students. When I use Grammarly to fix my writing, that feels different from using ChatGPT to write my essay. I think the line is somewhere around 'did I do the thinking?' but I'm honestly not sure where exactly."

🔍 The Reveal

All three samples were written by humans. Sample A was written by a teacher who was trying to sound "professional and balanced." It was flagged as AI-generated by two popular AI detection tools. Samples B and C were written authentically by practising teachers reflecting on their experience.

What AI detectors actually measure

AI detectors measure statistical patterns in text — specifically, how "predictable" each word choice is given the words that came before. Formal, structured writing — the kind teachers are often trained to produce — looks very similar to AI output by these metrics. Non-native speakers, students with certain writing styles, and anyone who has been taught to write "academically" are at elevated risk of false positive detection.

The evidence on accuracy

Major AI detection tools report false positive rates of 1–4% in controlled studies — but in real classroom conditions, rates are significantly higher for non-native speakers and for formal academic writing. A 2% false positive rate across 200 essays means 4 students wrongly suspected. No AI detector manufacturer recommends using their tool as the sole basis for an academic integrity decision.

The ethical problem with detectors

Using an AI detector as evidence in an integrity case creates a situation where a statistical probability is treated as proof — without the student's knowledge of what evidence is being used, or the ability to effectively challenge it. This is not a standard that would be accepted in any other professional context involving serious consequences for an individual.
