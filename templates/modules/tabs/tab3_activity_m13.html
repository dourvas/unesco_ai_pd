💡 How educators approach these grey zones:

On accuracy thresholds (Scenario 1)

Many educators draw the line based on purpose, not perfection. An image used as a discussion starter has a different accuracy threshold than one used as a reference diagram for an exam. The key is being transparent with students about the intended use. Pointing out the inaccuracy — as in the second option — actually creates a richer learning moment than a perfect image would.

On voice cloning and real people (Scenario 2)

The core issue is not the quality of the content — it is the identity claim. Using a voice that sounds like a specific real person, without their consent, creates an implicit claim that they said or endorsed something. Most professional ethics frameworks distinguish clearly between using AI voices generally (acceptable with disclosure) and mimicking identifiable individuals (requires consent or avoidance). The engagement benefit does not override this.

On disclosure after heavy editing (Scenario 3)

The question of "how much editing changes the obligation to disclose" does not have a universal answer. What most transparency frameworks agree on is this: the obligation to disclose is not primarily about how much of the final content the AI produced — it is about modelling the transparency you expect from students. If you are asking students to disclose their AI use, disclosing your own (even briefly) is consistent professional practice.
