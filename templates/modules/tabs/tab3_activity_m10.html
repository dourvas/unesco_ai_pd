💡 The correct annotations — and why the distractors don't work:

📌 Why

"The methodology link — reading text before map annotation — was the key decision. A generic explanation can precede any task. A map annotation task requires named, locatable places."

This reveals a pedagogical decision — the delivery context determined the content constraint. That is professional reasoning, not just a description of what happened.

💡 Surprise

"The AI knew Athens and Thessaloniki well but produced generic statements about 'rural areas.' Specifying Epirus by name produced much richer geographical detail — place specificity unlocked AI knowledge I hadn't expected."

This surfaces an assumption the teacher didn't know they were making — and produces a transferable insight: named specificity unlocks AI knowledge. That is what makes a surprise annotation valuable.

✗ Rejection

"I rejected an explanation attributing rural depopulation entirely to economic factors without mentioning terrain. Geographically incomplete — and it would have reinforced the idea that geography is just economics with maps."

This makes disciplinary judgment visible. The rejection isn't about quality — it's about what the subject requires. Only a geography teacher would know to make this call.

The distractors — why they don't count

The three distractor fragments describe outcomes (length was right, language was appropriate, topic choice was convenient) rather than reasoning. They tell colleagues what happened — not how the teacher was thinking. A boundary object requires the reasoning, not the result.
