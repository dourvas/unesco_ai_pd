# Resistance Matrix — M1–M5 (Acquire Level)
## Phase 3 of Subject Intro Hooks implementation

**Status:** Phase 3, batch 1 of 3 (M1–M5)
**Approach:** Y — group thinking, individual cells per subject
**Cells in this batch:** 5 modules × 17 = 85
**Next batches:** M6–M10 (Deepen), M11–M15 (Create)

---

## How to read this document

Each cell answers three questions for a (subject × module) pair:

- **R** (Resistance) — what this teacher might be silently thinking when they see this module
- **B** (Bridge) — the classroom-recognisable connection that makes the module their territory
- **P** (Promise) — the specific gain this teacher takes from this module

In Phase 4, each cell becomes one ~100-word hook displayed in TAB1.

The 17 cells per module are: 16 subjects + 1 Universal fallback.

---

## Subject group reference (for cross-cell consistency)

| Group | Subjects | Typical resistance posture |
|---|---|---|
| **STEM** | mathematics, physics, chemistry, biology, science, computer_science | Comfortable with technical concepts; sceptical about soft skills, ethics, identity |
| **Humanities** | language_arts, foreign_languages, history, social_studies, arts | Resists technical content; demands meaning, context, purpose |
| **Specialised** | physical_education, special_education, early_childhood, geography | "AI doesn't apply to my context" — feel ecosystem ignores them |
| **Generalist** | other (Primary Generalist) | Multi-subject + young learners — most underserved by AI ecosystem |
| **Universal** | (fallback only) | Catch-all for unmatched cases |

Within groups, cells share thinking patterns but never share final wording. Each cell is its own mini-essay in matrix form.

# M1 — Understanding AI in Education
## UNESCO Aspect 1, Acquire Level

**Module focus:** What is AI; how does it appear in education today; what changes for the teaching profession.

**Generic resistance pattern across all subjects:** "I'm here to teach my subject, not to become a technologist." The first module bears the heaviest doubt because the teacher hasn't yet seen any pedagogical payoff.

---

### M1 × mathematics
- **R:** AI is just clever statistics. I already teach statistics. What's new here?
- **B:** Mathematics teachers see the structure underneath AI more clearly than anyone. They can be the colleagues who explain it accurately to others, instead of repeating tech-press hype.
- **P:** Vocabulary and frame to talk about AI with mathematical accuracy, so you can answer the inevitable student questions without overclaiming or dismissing.

### M1 × language_arts
- **R:** AI writes. That threatens what I teach. Why would I want to learn more about it?
- **B:** Language teachers spend their careers helping students find their own voice. AI mimicry is the new contrast that makes voice teachable.
- **P:** Clear understanding of what AI can and can't do with language, so you stop arguing with students about whether it "knows what it's saying" and start using its limits as teaching material.

### M1 × science
- **R:** Another tech wave to add to coding, robotics, and STEM days. When does it stop?
- **B:** Science teachers have absorbed every tech wave for decades. They know what survives the hype and what doesn't, better than ed-tech vendors.
- **P:** Criteria to decide whether AI deserves a place in your science classroom, instead of being told it does by people who don't teach.

### M1 × physics
- **R:** Physics is governed by laws. AI is governed by training data. They live in different worlds.
- **B:** Physics teachers already train students to interrogate models and ask "where does this break?" That's exactly the question AI literacy needs.
- **P:** A way to position AI as a model to be tested, not a magic answer machine. Useful in any classroom that values falsifiability.

### M1 × chemistry
- **R:** I teach about reactions and mechanisms. AI feels like one more black box on top of black boxes.
- **B:** Chemistry teachers explain mechanisms behind apparent magic. AI is the next mechanism worth explaining, and your students will trust your version more than YouTube's.
- **P:** Enough understanding to decode AI's role in modern chemistry — molecular generation, lab automation, drug discovery — when students ask about it.

### M1 × biology
- **R:** AI in biology is biotech and bioinformatics. It's not really classroom material.
- **B:** Biology already deals with emergent behaviour, complex systems, neural networks (real ones). AI is built on biological metaphors that students recognise from your lessons.
- **P:** Confidence to make the brain → neural network bridge for students, accurately. Most teachers do it badly. You can do it well.

### M1 × history
- **R:** AI is a current event. I teach long-arc patterns. They don't connect.
- **B:** Every technology that reshaped knowledge — print, radio, internet — went through the same hype cycle. Historians spot it faster than anyone.
- **P:** A frame for placing AI in the long history of educational technology, so when colleagues panic or evangelise, you have the perspective to steady the conversation.

### M1 × geography
- **R:** Geography is grounded in real places, real people, real maps. AI is abstract and online.
- **B:** Geography teachers already use AI: it powers Google Maps, satellite analysis, climate models, traffic predictions. Most don't know the AI is there.
- **P:** Recognition of where AI already sits in your subject's tools, so you can name it for students instead of treating it as invisible infrastructure.

### M1 × foreign_languages
- **R:** Translation tools are getting too good. AI is taking over my subject. I'd rather not look.
- **B:** Translation has always been about more than word-substitution: register, idiom, cultural framing, intent. That's still entirely yours, and now more visible because AI makes the easy part trivial.
- **P:** A clear separation between what AI does well in language (surface) and where you remain irreplaceable (depth), so you teach with confidence rather than defensiveness.

### M1 × social_studies
- **R:** AI raises issues of bias, surveillance, power. Important but not my curriculum.
- **B:** Social studies already covers technology and society — industrial revolution, mass media, globalisation. AI is the current chapter of that exact story.
- **P:** A position on AI's social role that you've thought through, so when students ask "is this good or bad?" you can frame the question instead of dodging it.

### M1 × computer_science
- **R:** I teach this already. What can a generic teacher PD module add for me?
- **B:** Computer science teachers know the technology but rarely the pedagogy of the technology — how non-CS colleagues should think about it, what assumptions they bring, where the analogies break.
- **P:** Tools to be the AI explainer-in-residence at your school, supporting colleagues without dumbing down or losing them in jargon.

### M1 × physical_education
- **R:** AI and PE? I teach kids to move. There's no overlap.
- **B:** AI is already coaching elite athletes through video analysis, biomechanics, training load monitoring. The same tools are reaching schools.
- **P:** Awareness of how AI is entering sport and movement, so you can decide what to bring into your gym and what to keep human.

### M1 × arts
- **R:** AI generates images, music, writing. It threatens art-making itself. I'd rather not teach that.
- **B:** Art teachers have seen camera, photoshop, digital art each "end" art and not end it. Each tool became raw material.
- **P:** A framework for thinking about AI as an art-making material, not as a competitor, so you can decide on your own terms how it enters your studio.

### M1 × special_education
- **R:** AI tools are designed for typical learners. They never fit my students.
- **B:** Some of the most promising AI applications are accessibility tools: speech-to-text for students with motor challenges, image description for visual impairment, simplification for cognitive load.
- **P:** A scan of where AI genuinely helps in special education, separated from generic AI hype that ignores your students.

### M1 × early_childhood
- **R:** Young children need humans, songs, hands, dirt, books. AI doesn't belong in early years.
- **B:** AI literacy has to start somewhere, and what begins in early childhood — language, pattern recognition, story — is what AI mirrors. Children don't need to use AI to start understanding it.
- **P:** A position on AI in early years that you've worked out yourself, so you can answer parents who ask "should my four-year-old use ChatGPT?" with clarity.

### M1 × other (Primary Generalist)
- **R:** I teach maths, language, science, art, social studies, and PE to one class of children. AI tools are designed for secondary specialists with one subject. There's no time to learn five different AI workflows.
- **B:** Primary generalists actually have an advantage with AI literacy. You see the same students all day across all subjects. You can build cross-curricular AI awareness in ways no specialist can.
- **P:** A primary-school-shaped understanding of AI that respects your reality: limited time, broad curriculum, young children, and the obligation to model healthy tech relationships.

### M1 × Universal
- **R:** Whatever I teach, this looks like a tech module aimed at someone else.
- **B:** AI in education isn't a subject add-on. It's a shift in how knowledge is produced, consumed, and assessed across all subjects.
- **P:** A starting frame for your own thinking about AI in your classroom, before anyone else's framing locks in.

# M2 — AI Ethics for Educators
## UNESCO Aspect 2, Acquire Level

**Module focus:** Bias, fairness, transparency, privacy, student data, professional duty.

**Generic resistance pattern:** "Ethics sounds like compliance training. I'm a teacher, not a lawyer." Many teachers experience ethics as someone else's problem — the school's, the vendor's, the government's.

---

### M2 × mathematics
- **R:** Ethics belongs to philosophers and lawyers. I work with proofs.
- **B:** Mathematics is full of value-laden choices: what counts as elegant, which problems matter, who gets called "good at maths". The same ethical attention applies to AI.
- **P:** Ethics moves from abstract principles to concrete questions you can pose in a maths classroom — about fairness in grading algorithms, about who gets recommended for advanced classes.

### M2 × language_arts
- **R:** AI ethics is about plagiarism. I already deal with that.
- **B:** Beyond plagiarism: voice ownership, training-data origins, who gets erased when AI rewrites, whose dialect is treated as standard. All literary territory.
- **P:** A wider ethical map of AI in writing classrooms, so plagiarism becomes one issue among many rather than the whole conversation.

### M2 × science
- **R:** Science values evidence and replicability. I'm objective. Ethics is for other subjects.
- **B:** Scientific objectivity is itself an ethical stance, and AI's claim to objectivity hides specific value choices. Science teachers are best placed to teach students to interrogate that claim.
- **P:** Ethics becomes part of scientific literacy, not a separate module bolted on. Useful in your existing curriculum, not an add-on.

### M2 × physics
- **R:** Physics doesn't do values. AI ethics is sociology dressed up.
- **B:** Physics teaches measurement uncertainty and observer effects. AI ethics asks the same questions: what is being measured, by whom, with what error, affecting whom.
- **P:** A way to bring AI ethics into physics through measurement and modelling decisions, without abandoning what makes physics rigorous.

### M2 × chemistry
- **R:** My ethics framework is lab safety and academic integrity. That's enough.
- **B:** Chemistry has long ethics practice: dual-use research, environmental impact, drug development. AI raises related questions in your students' lives.
- **P:** Ethics expanded from lab to algorithm, using frames you already teach. Continuity, not new vocabulary.

### M2 × biology
- **R:** Biology ethics is about life, not algorithms. They're not comparable.
- **B:** AI bias in healthcare, genomics, environmental modelling — all of it intersects biology classrooms. Students will face these issues whether or not the curriculum names them.
- **P:** Biological ethics extended into AI-mediated biology, giving you classroom material for the ethical lessons your students will live in adulthood.

### M2 × history
- **R:** History is full of ethics. AI ethics is just the latest chapter, not a separate field.
- **B:** Exactly the right view, and a powerful starting point. History teachers are uniquely positioned to refuse the framing of AI ethics as unprecedented and instead teach it through pattern-recognition.
- **P:** A toolkit for teaching AI ethics through historical analogy: who wrote the rules, who benefited, who was excluded. Pre-existing pedagogy, new examples.

### M2 × geography
- **R:** Privacy and bias in AI sound like online problems. Geography is about places.
- **B:** AI is reshaping geography directly: location surveillance, predictive policing, climate modelling whose assumptions affect real communities. Place-based ethics has new urgency.
- **P:** Geographic concepts — territory, access, scale, vulnerability — become the lens for AI ethics. Your subject becomes the analytical home for these questions.

### M2 × foreign_languages
- **R:** Ethics in language teaching is about respect across cultures. AI doesn't change that.
- **B:** AI absolutely changes it. Whose languages are well-represented in training data; whose voices are simplified, stereotyped, or erased; how translation flattens nuance. These are language-teacher questions.
- **P:** A specifically linguistic frame for AI ethics, where bias isn't only social but also lexical, syntactic, and dialectal — territory you already cover.

### M2 × social_studies
- **R:** AI ethics is part of my curriculum already. I might know more than this module assumes.
- **B:** Possibly so. But the operationalisation in classrooms — how to teach it without lecturing, how to build student agency, how to set classroom AI norms — is still being worked out.
- **P:** Less theory, more tested classroom practice. The module respects what you bring and adds tools you can deploy on Monday.

### M2 × computer_science
- **R:** I taught ethics last term. What's new?
- **B:** Most CS ethics curricula focus on developer ethics. Teacher ethics is different: you're not building the system, you're deciding whether to inflict it on students.
- **P:** A teacher-specific ethical lens that complements the developer-specific one your CS curriculum already covers.

### M2 × physical_education
- **R:** PE ethics is about fairness in sport, body image, inclusion. AI doesn't enter.
- **B:** AI enters PE through fitness trackers, body composition apps, performance video analysis — all collecting data on minors. Ethics gets concrete fast.
- **P:** A PE-specific ethics conversation about student bodies and AI surveillance, before the tech reaches your gym uninvited.

### M2 × arts
- **R:** AI ethics in art is the training-data theft debate. I've heard it.
- **B:** Yes, but also: emotional manipulation through generative content, deepfakes affecting students, identity and authorship in collaborative AI work. Wider than data ethics alone.
- **P:** An art-classroom ethics framework that treats AI as material with provenance, consequences, and meaning, not just as a copyright case.

### M2 × special_education
- **R:** Ethics for my students means dignity and individualised support. AI ethics feels parallel.
- **B:** AI bias hurts learners with disabilities specifically: speech recognition that fails non-standard speech, accessibility tools that make assumptions, "personalised learning" trained on neurotypical data.
- **P:** Ethics tools to evaluate AI products you might be sold, with your students' particular vulnerabilities in focus.

### M2 × early_childhood
- **R:** My ethics is about safeguarding, attachment, healthy development. AI is too distant to matter.
- **B:** AI products targeting young children — voice toys, "educational" apps, screen-time recommenders — already shape what reaches your classroom. Your ethics framework is the front line.
- **P:** Specific criteria for evaluating AI products before they enter your early-years setting, drawn from your existing safeguarding language.

### M2 × other (Primary Generalist)
- **R:** I deal with ethics every day across every subject — friendship rules, fairness, kindness. AI ethics seems like one more abstract topic with no time to fit it in.
- **B:** Primary classrooms are where ethical instincts are formed. AI ethics for ten-year-olds is exactly fairness, honesty, kindness — applied to a new technology. You have the relational base; the topic is just new vocabulary.
- **P:** A primary-classroom-friendly version of AI ethics, integrated into the values teaching you already do, not a separate curriculum strand.

### M2 × Universal
- **R:** Ethics modules tend to be either too abstract or too compliance-focused. Probably this one too.
- **B:** Teacher ethics is neither — it's the daily judgement of what's fair and right in your classroom. AI just creates new versions of old questions.
- **P:** A practical ethics frame for AI decisions you'll actually make: about tools, about student data, about your own use of AI in lesson preparation.

# M3 — AI Foundations: How It Works
## UNESCO Aspect 3, Acquire Level

**Module focus:** What's actually happening when AI generates text, images, predictions; tokens, training, models, limits.

**Generic resistance pattern:** "This is computer science. I'm not an engineer." This module faces the strongest "wrong audience" reaction because it sounds technical from the title alone.

---

### M3 × mathematics
- **R:** I'll spot the maths underneath quickly. Hopefully not too watered down.
- **B:** This module respects mathematical literacy. It uses vectors, probabilities, optimisation in ways a maths teacher will recognise and possibly enjoy.
- **P:** A clean mental model of how transformers work, from a maths point of view, that you can adapt for your students at the right level.

### M3 × language_arts
- **R:** Tokens, embeddings, transformers. All Greek to me. (And not the good kind.)
- **B:** LLMs are language tools first. They tokenise, model context, predict next words — exactly what a language teacher already thinks about, in different vocabulary. You already speak this language; you just call it morphology, syntax, semantics.
- **P:** Enough technical understanding to know why AI struggles with long literary texts (context windows) and ambiguity (probability), so you can use it intentionally rather than blindly.

### M3 × science
- **R:** A black box. Worse than the ones I already complain about in textbooks.
- **B:** Science teachers don't accept "it just works." This module gives you enough internal mechanism to teach AI as a system to be questioned, not an oracle to consult.
- **P:** A scientifically respectable model of how AI produces output, suitable for telling students "here's what's actually happening, not magic."

### M3 × physics
- **R:** I'll see if it's correct or hand-wavy. Most pop-AI explanations are.
- **B:** This module accepts that physics teachers want accuracy. It does not pretend gradient descent is rolling down a hill. It treats the maths as the maths.
- **P:** A physics-grade understanding of training and inference, which lets you decide what to bring into your classroom and what to leave for the textbook.

### M3 × chemistry
- **R:** Mechanisms in AI must look very different from chemical mechanisms.
- **B:** Surprisingly similar in structure: layered transformations, energy minimisation, equilibria. Different domain, related thinking.
- **P:** A way to use chemistry's mechanism-thinking habit to grasp AI internals, instead of starting from scratch with unfamiliar metaphors.

### M3 × biology
- **R:** Neural networks aren't really like brains. The metaphor is fake.
- **B:** Correct, and worth saying out loud. The module clarifies where the brain analogy holds and where it breaks. Biology teachers need that distinction more than anyone, because their students will misunderstand otherwise.
- **P:** Precision about what AI shares with biological cognition (statistical learning) and what it doesn't (embodiment, drives, meaning). Useful when teaching nervous systems too.

### M3 × history
- **R:** Technical innards of AI feel disconnected from what I teach.
- **B:** History of AI itself is in here: symbolic AI to neural networks to transformers, each shift driven by who funded research and what data became available. A historian will find friends in this story.
- **P:** Historical perspective on AI's intellectual development, so when you talk to students about the current AI moment you can locate it in time, not eternity.

### M3 × geography
- **R:** I'm surprised AI foundations are even relevant to geography.
- **B:** They are: most geographic AI uses the same underlying models, applied to spatial data. Image classification on satellite imagery, for example, is the same architecture as image classification on cats.
- **P:** Recognition that geography's AI tools share architecture with chatbots, which means understanding one helps you understand the other. One module covers two domains.

### M3 × foreign_languages
- **R:** This is going to be tokens and probabilities. Numbers, not languages.
- **B:** It is, but the framing is linguistic: how does a model represent a word, why does it confuse polysemes, why does it lose context across long passages. Language teachers find these questions familiar.
- **P:** Technical vocabulary to explain to students why machine translation produces certain errors — and to use those errors as language-learning moments.

### M3 × social_studies
- **R:** I want the social side of AI, not the technical innards.
- **B:** The technical innards explain the social effects. Bias comes from training data, which comes from human-generated content, which carries social patterns. The pipeline is the politics.
- **P:** Technical literacy that strengthens your social analysis instead of being separate from it. Better equipped to teach algorithmic accountability.

### M3 × computer_science
- **R:** I'll know most of this. Hoping for at least one new framing.
- **B:** Likely true. The value here is the teacher framing: how to introduce these concepts to non-CS colleagues and students without losing them in jargon or oversimplifying.
- **P:** Pedagogical scaffolding for ideas you already understand, ready to deploy with non-specialist audiences.

### M3 × physical_education
- **R:** AI foundations? In PE? Skipping this.
- **B:** Stay a moment. AI in PE means motion analysis, performance prediction, even biomechanics. Foundations matter when you're deciding whether to trust an app's verdict on a student's movement.
- **P:** Enough understanding to evaluate the AI tools entering sport and movement, instead of accepting whatever vendors claim about accuracy.

### M3 × arts
- **R:** Inside-the-machine view of generative AI. Not what art is about.
- **B:** Knowing the medium matters in art. A painter understands pigment, a photographer understands optics. AI artists who don't understand training data are missing the medium.
- **P:** Material knowledge of generative AI that lets you teach it as a craft with affordances and limits, not as a button that produces images.

### M3 × special_education
- **R:** Technical AI module. I need pedagogical practice.
- **B:** The technical side matters in special education specifically. Whether a speech recognition tool will work for your student depends on whose voices were in the training data. Foundation knowledge tells you what to ask vendors.
- **P:** Vocabulary and concepts to interrogate AI products on behalf of your students before adopting them.

### M3 × early_childhood
- **R:** Foundations of AI seems even more remote from my classroom than the AI itself.
- **B:** Children form their first models of how AI works through what adults around them say. Your accuracy matters when a parent asks you why ChatGPT got something wrong.
- **P:** Enough technical understanding to give children, parents, and colleagues honest, age-appropriate explanations of what AI actually does.

### M3 × other (Primary Generalist)
- **R:** I'm not a maths or computer science teacher. Tokens, transformers, embeddings — I'd need a course just to understand the words.
- **B:** Primary generalists explain how everything works in simple terms every day, from photosynthesis to fractions to friendships. AI is one more thing to understand at the level you can re-explain.
- **P:** A primary-school-friendly mental model of AI that you can use across subjects: in language work when discussing text generation, in science when discussing pattern recognition, in social studies when discussing fairness.

### M3 × Universal
- **R:** A technical module. This will be the slog.
- **B:** Foundations are background knowledge, like geography or grammar. You don't need to master the maths, just understand the shape of what's happening when AI produces output.
- **P:** A non-technical-but-honest model of how AI works, sufficient to make informed decisions in your classroom and resist both overclaiming and panicking.

# M4 — AI in the Pedagogical Toolkit
## UNESCO Aspect 4, Acquire Level

**Module focus:** Practical AI uses in lesson planning, differentiation, feedback, formative assessment.

**Generic resistance pattern:** "Yet another tool." Teachers have been promised efficiency by every ed-tech wave for thirty years. Resistance is fatigue, not rejection.

---

### M4 × mathematics
- **R:** I have my own ways of generating problem sets. Why outsource that?
- **B:** AI is poor at generating mathematically interesting problems. But it's good at varying surface features (numbers, contexts) on problems you've already designed. That frees you for the structural work only you can do.
- **P:** A specific division of labour where AI handles the generative grunt work and you keep the design judgement, freeing time for what you actually trained for.

### M4 × language_arts
- **R:** AI marking student writing? That's the worst possible use of it.
- **B:** Agreed for summative assessment. But for low-stakes formative feedback — sentence-level clarity, word choice, structural diagnosis — AI scales what one teacher can't.
- **P:** A sharp distinction between AI feedback uses you'll trust (formative, draft-stage) and ones you'll refuse (summative, judgemental), with classroom workflows for each.

### M4 × science
- **R:** Lesson planning shortcuts often produce wrong science. I'd rather write my own.
- **B:** True for content. But AI is decent at scaffolding investigations, generating misconception-rich examples, drafting student-friendly explanations of your accurate ones. You stay in charge of accuracy; AI multiplies your reach.
- **P:** A workflow where AI never decides the science but helps you produce more material around what you've already got right.

### M4 × physics
- **R:** Most AI examples in physics are wrong or trivial. I checked.
- **B:** They are. The module assumes that and focuses on uses that don't depend on AI knowing physics: differentiation of existing material, generating worked-example variations, drafting prerequisite checks.
- **P:** AI uses that survive physics-teacher scrutiny because they don't require the AI to actually know physics.

### M4 × chemistry
- **R:** Chemistry teaching depends on accuracy and safety. AI introduces both risks.
- **B:** True. The module's chemistry uses are bounded: differentiating language complexity in your existing materials, generating quiz distractors based on your taught misconceptions, drafting parent communications. None of it touches reaction predictions.
- **P:** A safe-zone map of where AI helps in chemistry teaching without putting students at chemical risk.

### M4 × biology
- **R:** Biology is full of detail. AI confidently produces wrong details. Hard pass.
- **B:** Confidence is the problem; the module addresses it head-on. AI uses in biology: differentiating reading levels of a paper you've already vetted, drafting visual descriptions for accessibility, summarising your own lecture notes for revision sheets. Not generating biology.
- **P:** Specific uses where the AI works on your verified content rather than producing biology from scratch.

### M4 × history
- **R:** AI hallucinates historical detail spectacularly. Why would I let it near my classroom?
- **B:** You wouldn't, for content. The module's history uses are different: generating period-appropriate primary-source analysis questions for sources you've selected, drafting differentiated reading levels of textbook passages you've vetted, creating debate prompts.
- **P:** History uses that route around AI's hallucination problem rather than depending on AI to know history.

### M4 × geography
- **R:** Geography is local and current. AI training data is global and old.
- **B:** Exactly the constraint. The module's geography uses don't ask AI for current local facts. They ask it to draft fieldwork preparation, generate sketch-mapping prompts, produce differentiated atlas-reading questions.
- **P:** Geography uses tuned to what AI is bad at (current local data) and what it's good at (process scaffolding).

### M4 × foreign_languages
- **R:** AI in language teaching feels like having students cheat in slow motion.
- **B:** Cheating isn't using AI; it's using AI without cognitive engagement. The module separates lazy uses (AI does the work) from generative uses (AI provides scaffolding for student production).
- **P:** A teacher's playbook for distinguishing AI-as-shortcut from AI-as-scaffolding in language learning, with classroom routines that protect engagement.

### M4 × social_studies
- **R:** Lesson planning AI seems harmless. The bigger issue is what students do with AI.
- **B:** Both matter. The module covers your prep workflow first, since teaching with AI starts with thinking through your own use of it. Student-facing comes in M9 and M14.
- **P:** Time-saving prep workflows now; classroom student practices later. Modular and not overwhelming.

### M4 × computer_science
- **R:** AI for lesson planning. Sure. What CS-specific uses are covered?
- **B:** CS-specific uses include: generating beginner-friendly explanations of code you've selected, producing variation problems with similar structure but different surface, drafting commit-by-commit walkthroughs.
- **P:** CS-pedagogy uses tuned to teaching programming to students who haven't yet developed code-reading habits.

### M4 × physical_education
- **R:** AI helping me plan PE lessons? Most online plans are nonsense already.
- **B:** True. The module's PE uses are narrow: drafting differentiated rule explanations for inclusive games, producing risk-assessment language for parents, generating warm-up variations for rotation.
- **P:** Modest, useful PE applications focused on the paperwork around the practice, not the practice itself.

### M4 × arts
- **R:** AI in art education must mean image generation. I'd rather not.
- **B:** That comes in M13. This module is about your prep: drafting artist-statement scaffolds, varying difficulty levels of critique prompts, generating gallery-tour discussion questions.
- **P:** Prep-time uses that don't ask you to take a position on student AI image generation. Those decisions stay yours, untouched by this module.

### M4 × special_education
- **R:** Generic AI tools never accommodate. Adaptation always falls on me.
- **B:** Mostly true. The module's special-ed angle is using AI to lessen your adaptation workload: differentiating reading levels of your materials, generating visual schedules from your lesson plans, drafting parent communications in plain language.
- **P:** AI as a labour multiplier for the adaptation work you're already doing, not as a replacement for your professional judgement.

### M4 × early_childhood
- **R:** Early years prep is observation notes, learning stories, parent communication. None of that is AI work.
- **B:** All of that is exactly AI work, if you want it to be: AI is decent at drafting parent communications from your bullet-point observations, producing learning-story templates, suggesting follow-up activities for emerging interests.
- **P:** Time-saving applications that respect your professional judgement (you decide what matters) while accelerating the writing-up work.

### M4 × other (Primary Generalist)
- **R:** I plan five subjects a week. If AI saves me time in one subject, I lose it learning the AI in another. Net zero.
- **B:** Generalists actually benefit most from AI prep tools because the same AI workflow scales across subjects. Learn it once for spelling lists, deploy it for science vocabulary, art prompts, maths word problems.
- **P:** A small set of cross-curricular AI workflows specifically tuned to multi-subject planning, where the time-saving compounds across your week.

### M4 × Universal
- **R:** Another tool to learn. I've been promised efficiency before.
- **B:** Reasonable scepticism. The module shows specific, narrow uses and is honest about what isn't worth the trouble.
- **P:** A short, specific list of AI uses that survive teacher scepticism, plus equally honest warnings about uses that don't pay off.

# M5 — Reflective Prompt Engineering: An Introduction
## UNESCO Aspect 5, Acquire Level

**Module focus:** Prompting as a reflective professional practice (RPE Framework). Introduction to the seven strategies. EduPrompt Studio is introduced more deeply in M8.

**Generic resistance pattern:** "Prompt engineering is a tech skill. I shouldn't need to learn how to talk to a machine." Teachers often see prompting as either trivial (just type a question) or alien (a programming task).

---

### M5 × mathematics
- **R:** Prompting is just clear writing. Why dress it up as engineering?
- **B:** It's clear writing about pedagogical intent — closer to writing a good problem statement than to coding. Maths teachers already do this when they design assessments.
- **P:** A way to externalise your pedagogical thinking as you prompt, which sharpens both the prompt and your awareness of what you're trying to teach.
